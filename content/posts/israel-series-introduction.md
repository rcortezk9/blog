Something is happening in American Christianity that has no name in the tradition.

A branch of Protestant theology — one invented in the nineteenth century, unknown to the Church Fathers, absent from every council and synod of the first eighteen centuries of Christian history — has become the assumed framework for how millions of Christians interpret the Old Testament, read the news, and form their political obligations. It teaches that ethnic identity, not faith, determines one's standing before God. It teaches that a modern nation-state is the fulfillment of biblical prophecy. It teaches that Christians bear a sacred duty to support that state's policies — not as a personal conviction but as an exegetical conclusion. It calls this "biblical Christianity."

It is not.

---

This series is not about politics. It is not about any particular nation or people. I want to say that plainly — not as a disclaimer, but as a statement of what this series is actually about: a theological question. Who are the covenant people of God? How does one enter the covenant? What does St. Paul mean when he writes of Israel, of Abraham's seed, of the circumcision of the heart? The critique here is aimed at a theological system, not at a people. The Orthodox tradition loves the Jewish people enough to hold open the eschatological promise of Romans 11 for them — to this day, reverently, without turning it into a political program.

---

I am a catechumen in the Orthodox Church, preparing for Baptism and Chrismation. The question of Israel and the Church was not, for me, an academic curiosity. It was a crisis of conscience — watching a theological system dress a political agenda in the language of prophecy, and finding that I had no response to it from within the Christianity I had been given.

The Orthodox Church gave me one. Not a new one. The same one the Church has always held: that Israel's vocation was always fulfilled in Christ, that the covenant people are constituted by faith and the sacramental life of the Body, and that the mystery of Romans 11 is held with reverence as a sacred promise awaiting God's hour — not explained away, not turned into an ideology, but received.

That is what this series is about. I did not construct a novel position. I found a tradition that had been holding the honest answer for two thousand years before I came looking for it.
