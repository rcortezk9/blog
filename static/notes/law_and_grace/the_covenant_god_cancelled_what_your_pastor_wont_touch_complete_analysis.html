The Covenant God Cancelled: What Your Pastor Won't Touch - Complete Analysis

Video Information

  • Speaker: Sam Shamoun
  • Channel: Shamounian Archive
  • Duration: ~25 minutes
  • Date: November 17, 2025
  • Primary Scripture: Jeremiah 18:1-12, Hebrews 8, Romans 9-11, John 15

Section Overview

This teaching represents a forceful and comprehensive challenge to Christian Zionism and Dispensational theology, presenting instead a Reformed/Covenant theology understanding of God's relationship with ethnic Israel and the Church. Sam Shamoun systematically deconstructs what he identifies as widespread biblical misinterpretation regarding God's promises to Israel, arguing that pastors avoid these truths due to political correctness and contemporary Zionist sympathies. The presentation unfolds through three strategic movements: first, establishing from Jeremiah 18 that God's promises to nations—including Israel—are fundamentally conditional upon obedience; second, demonstrating from Hebrews 8 and the New Testament that the Old Covenant has been made obsolete due to Israel's failure to meet those conditions; and third, redefining "Israel" through Romans 9-11 and John 15 as all believers in Christ (both Jew and Gentile) rather than ethnic descendants of Jacob.

The teaching's significance lies in its direct confrontation with popular evangelical positions on modern Israel, the land promise, and end-times prophecy. Shamoun doesn't merely present an alternative interpretation; he challenges the biblical literacy and theological integrity of those who teach unconditional promises to ethnic Israel, repeatedly demanding that opponents "show me where the New Testament says" Israel must possess the land. His methodology combines extensive Scripture quotation, pointed rhetorical questions, historical-theological arguments from the church fathers, and passionate polemic against what he calls "Bible butchers" and "ass-kissing Zionists." The presentation moves from exegetical foundation (Jeremiah 18's potter and clay) through apostolic interpretation (Paul in Romans, the author of Hebrews) to Christological centerpiece (Jesus as the olive tree/vine, not Israel).

This section functions as both defensive and offensive apologetics—defensive in protecting orthodox covenant theology from what Shamoun sees as modern innovations that idolize ethnic Israel, offensive in actively refuting Christian Zionism's biblical foundations and challenging listeners to examine whether their eschatology is driven by Scripture or by contemporary politics. The teaching's practical implications extend to how Christians view modern Israel's establishment in 1948, support for Israeli politics, evangelism of Jews, and understanding of their own identity as the "Israel of God."

Detailed Point Analysis

Main Point 1: God's Promises to Israel Were Conditional, Not Unconditional

Core Argument: The foundational error in Christian Zionism and much contemporary evangelical eschatology lies in treating God's promises to Israel as unconditional guarantees that must be fulfilled regardless of Israel's behavior. Shamoun demolishes this position by directing attention to Jeremiah 18:1-12, a passage he argues is systematically ignored by those who teach unconditional promises. This text presents the potter and clay analogy, where God explicitly declares His sovereign right to alter His plans for nations based on their response to Him. The critical verses establish a bilateral principle: "If that nation against which I have spoken turns from its evil, I will relent of the disaster I thought to bring upon it" (conditional blessing), and conversely, "If it does evil in my sight by not listening to my voice, then I will relent concerning the good which I promised to do to it" (conditional curse). This means that even divinely promised blessings can be revoked if the recipients fail to meet the stated or implied conditions. Applied to Israel, this means the land promises in Jeremiah 31:35-40 and 33:14-26, the covenant of Levitical priesthood, and other national blessings were all subject to Israel's obedience—conditions they catastrophically failed to meet by rejecting and executing the Messiah.

Historical Context: Understanding conditionality in ancient Near Eastern covenants illuminates this teaching. Suzerain-vassal treaties of the biblical period routinely included both stipulations (what the vassal must do) and curses/blessings (consequences of obedience or disobedience). The Mosaic Covenant followed this pattern explicitly in Deuteronomy 28-30, with detailed blessings for obedience and horrific curses for disobedience, culminating in exile from the land. This wasn't abstract theology but lived reality: Israel experienced exactly these covenant curses through the Assyrian destruction of the northern kingdom (722 BC) and the Babylonian exile of Judah (586 BC). When Jeremiah prophesied in the late 7th and early 6th centuries BC, he was speaking to a nation already experiencing God's judgment for covenant breaking. His potter and clay message (chapter 18) comes in the context of imminent judgment, yet even then offered hope if Israel would repent. The principle Shamoun emphasizes—that God's promises can be revoked based on response—wasn't theoretical but was being demonstrated in real time through Israel's history. This historical pattern makes the New Testament's testimony that God "found fault with them" and "made the covenant obsolete" (Hebrews 8) not a surprising innovation but the expected outcome of persistent covenant breaking.

Biblical Foundation: The scriptural principle underlying this point is the consistency of God's character in dealing with covenant partners throughout redemptive history. God is not capricious—He doesn't arbitrarily change His mind. But neither is He trapped by His own words when the recipients flagrantly violate the covenant terms. The pattern appears repeatedly: God warned Adam and Eve of consequences for disobedience; they disobeyed and faced those consequences. God warned antediluvian humanity; they persisted in evil, and judgment came through the flood. God promised Eli's family a perpetual priesthood (1 Samuel 2:30), then explicitly revoked it because of their sin, stating "Far be it from me, for those who honor me I will honor, and those who despise me shall be lightly esteemed." This last example is particularly instructive—an explicitly "perpetual" promise was revoked due to unfaithfulness, establishing the precedent that God's promises carry implicit or explicit conditions even when they seem absolute. The principle extends to the New Covenant as well: Jesus warns branches that don't bear fruit will be cut off and burned (John 15:6), and Paul warns Gentile believers they can be cut off from the olive tree if they don't continue in faith (Romans 11:21-22). Conditionality is not an exception but the rule in God's covenantal dealings.

Argument Development: This point establishes the hermeneutical and theological foundation for everything that follows in Shamoun's presentation. By proving from Jeremiah 18 that promises to nations are conditional, he undermines the entire edifice of Christian Zionist eschatology, which depends on unconditional, irrevocable promises to ethnic Israel. The logic unfolds systematically: (1) Jeremiah 18 establishes divine prerogative to change plans based on national response; (2) Israel responded with persistent rebellion, culminating in deicide; (3) therefore God was perfectly just and consistent with His revealed character in making the old covenant obsolete and transferring covenant blessings to the new Israel (the Church, composed of believing Jews and Gentiles). The argument addresses not just isolated promises but the entire framework of how God's word operates—it's effective and powerful, but it accomplishes God's purposes, which may include judgment and revocation when appropriate, not mechanical fulfillment regardless of circumstances. This sets up Shamoun's challenge throughout the lecture: show where the New Testament affirms these Old Testament promises to ethnic Israel are still in effect. If they were unconditional, such affirmation should be abundant; its absence is telling.

Practical Implications: This understanding profoundly affects how Christians read their Bibles and construct their theology. It demands careful attention to covenant structure—identifying stipulations, recognizing implicit conditions, and acknowledging that God's stated intentions may change based on human response. This applies not just to national promises but to individual ones: God's general promises of blessing are conditioned on faithfulness, obedience, and perseverance. The teaching also provides a framework for understanding difficult passages where God seems to change His mind or relent—this isn't divine fickleness but covenant faithfulness, where God responds appropriately to human actions. Practically, this means Christians should test eschatological systems against this principle: does this interpretation account for conditionality? Does it explain what happened when conditions weren't met? Does it allow the New Testament to interpret and potentially modify Old Testament expectations? A system that ignores Jeremiah 18's explicit teaching fails at the foundational level. The teaching also warns against presumption—whether national (ethnic Israel assuming God must bless them regardless of behavior) or individual (Christians assuming "once saved always saved" despite biblical warnings about falling away).

Analogy: Consider a parent who promises their child, "If you keep your room clean all semester, I'll buy you that bicycle you want for your birthday." This is a genuine promise—the parent fully intends to fulfill it and has the means to do so. The child starts strong, cleaning diligently for a few weeks. But gradually, the room becomes messier, clothes pile up, and eventually it's a disaster. The parent reminds the child multiple times, offering chances to correct course. The child promises to do better but doesn't follow through. When the birthday arrives, would we say the parent is "breaking their promise" by not providing the bicycle? No—the promise was conditional upon sustained obedience, and the condition wasn't met. The parent's word was faithful; the child's response was unfaithful. Now imagine the child saying, "But you promised! You can't back out now!" and demanding the bicycle despite flagrant disobedience. We'd recognize this as manipulation and entitlement. Similarly, when Christian Zionists insist God must fulfill land promises to an Israel that rejected the Messiah, they're treating God's conditional promises as if they were unconditional guarantees, missing the explicit teaching of Jeremiah 18 that God will "relent concerning the good" He promised if the nation does evil.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: The Potter and Clay Analogy Reveals God's Sovereign Flexibility

Jeremiah 18:1-6 presents a vivid picture that would have been immediately recognizable to ancient audiences: a potter working clay on a wheel. The vessel becomes marred—perhaps due to an impurity in the clay, an air bubble, or the clay's failure to respond properly to the potter's hands. The potter doesn't discard the clay or accept a flawed vessel; instead, he reworks it into a different vessel "as seemed good to the potter to make." This analogy illustrates several crucial principles. First, God's purpose is always to create something good and beautiful—His intention toward Israel was blessing, not destruction. Second, sometimes the clay "spoils in the potter's hand"—not because the potter is incompetent, but because the clay has flaws or doesn't respond properly. Third, when this happens, the potter doesn't abandon his purpose but achieves it through a different design. Applied to Israel, God's original design was to bless them, give them the land, establish them as a light to nations, and bring the Messiah through their lineage. But Israel "spoiled" in His hands through persistent rebellion. So God didn't abandon His redemptive purpose; He reshaped the plan—making the covenant obsolete, bringing in the Gentiles, and defining true Israel as all believers in Christ. The clay (ethnic Israel) continues to exist, but the vessel's design has changed from national-political to spiritual-universal.

Sub-point B: Israel's Ultimate Failure Was Rejecting the Messiah

While Israel had a long history of covenant breaking—idolatry, injustice, ignoring the prophets—Shamoun identifies their climactic failure as handing over the God-man to be executed. This wasn't merely one more sin in a long list; it was the definitive repudiation of God's covenant purposes. Jesus Himself pronounced judgment on this generation: "Therefore I tell you, the kingdom of God will be taken away from you and given to a people producing its fruits" (Matthew 21:43). He prophesied the destruction of Jerusalem and the temple (Matthew 24), which occurred in 70 AD—a visible, historical demonstration that God had indeed made the old covenant obsolete and executed judgment on covenant-breaking Israel. The teaching emphasizes that this wasn't arbitrary divine wrath but the logical outworking of Jeremiah 18's principle: Israel did the ultimate evil by killing the Messiah, so God relented concerning the good He had promised to do for them as a nation. This doesn't mean individual Jews can't be saved (Shamoun affirms they can and many are), but it means the national, political, territorial promises associated with the old covenant are no longer in effect.

Main Point 2: The Old Covenant Has Been Made Obsolete

Core Argument: Moving from the conditional nature of promises to their actual status, Shamoun presents the New Testament's explicit teaching that the old covenant is obsolete. The key text is Hebrews 8, which quotes Jeremiah 31:31-34 about the new covenant, then draws the conclusion: "For if that first covenant had been faultless, there would have been no occasion sought for a second." The passage clarifies that the fault was not with God or with the covenant itself, but with the people: "For finding fault with them, he says..." This distinction is crucial—the Mosaic covenant was a good and holy covenant (Romans 7:12), but it was powerless to accomplish its purpose because the people couldn't and wouldn't keep it. Therefore, God instituted a new and better covenant, and in doing so, "made the first obsolete." The Greek word translated "obsolete" (palaiōn) carries the sense of something made old, worn out, antiquated, no longer useful for its intended purpose. What's more, the verse continues: "And what is becoming obsolete and growing old is ready to vanish away." When Hebrews was written, the temple still stood and Levitical sacrifices continued, but the author prophetically announces these are "ready to vanish"—which they did in 70 AD when Rome destroyed Jerusalem and the temple, ending the sacrificial system permanently. This isn't replacement theology in the sense of the Church replacing Israel, but fulfillment theology—the old finds its completion and surpassing in the new.

Historical Context: The book of Hebrews was written to Jewish Christians (or Jews considering Christ) who were facing pressure to return to Judaism, likely due to persecution from both Jewish and Roman authorities. These believers were steeped in Old Testament worship, temple rituals, priesthood, and covenant thinking. The author's purpose throughout the epistle is to demonstrate that Christ and the new covenant are superior to everything in the old system: better than angels (chapter 1), better than Moses (chapter 3), better rest than Canaan (chapter 4), better priesthood than Aaron (chapters 5-7), better covenant (chapter 8), better sacrifice (chapters 9-10), better promises (8:6). This isn't denigrating the Old Testament—these things were good, God-ordained, and served their purpose. But they were shadows pointing to the reality found in Christ (10:1). When the reality arrives, the shadow's purpose is fulfilled. The historical crisis these believers faced—watching the old system potentially destroyed—is reinterpreted by Hebrews as divine confirmation of the gospel. The destruction of the temple wasn't a tragedy undermining God's promises but the visible demonstration that God had indeed moved on to the new covenant. The Levitical priesthood hasn't functioned for over 1,900 years, exactly as Hebrews predicted. This historical fact confirms the theological claim: the old covenant is obsolete.

Biblical Foundation: The principle underlying this point is progressive revelation—God's redemptive plan unfolds in stages, with later revelation building upon, interpreting, and sometimes superseding earlier revelation. This doesn't mean God changes or that earlier revelation was false, but that God's pedagogical method involves progressive disclosure. Paul illustrates this in Galatians 3:24-25, describing the law as a "tutor" (paidagōgos) to lead us to Christ, "but now that faith has come, we are no longer under a tutor." Just as a child graduates from elementary to advanced studies, leaving behind what was appropriate for an earlier stage, so God's people have graduated from the preparatory covenant to the fulfillment covenant. The old covenant served essential purposes: revealing sin (Romans 3:20), restraining evil (1 Timothy 1:9-10), providing types and shadows of Christ (Colossians 2:16-17), preserving the messianic line (Galatians 3:23-24), and preparing the world for the gospel (Galatians 4:4). But when Christ came, these preparatory purposes were accomplished. Attempting to maintain the old covenant system after Christ is like insisting a college student continue attending kindergarten—it misunderstands the purpose of the earlier stage and rejects the advancement God has provided. The obsolescence of the old covenant doesn't dishonor it but recognizes it accomplished exactly what God intended it to accomplish.

Argument Development: This point builds directly on Point 1 by showing that the conditional nature of the promises played out exactly as Jeremiah 18 predicted it would. Israel failed to meet the conditions (Point 1), therefore God made the covenant obsolete (Point 2). The argument chain is air-tight: (1) Promises were conditional (Jeremiah 18); (2) Conditions weren't met (Israel's persistent rebellion and deicide); (3) Therefore God exercised His prerogative to change plans (making the covenant obsolete, Hebrews 8); (4) This was confirmed historically (temple destruction, end of Levitical priesthood); (5) The New Testament explicitly states this (Hebrews 8, Romans 11, Galatians 4). Shamoun challenges opponents to explain how they can maintain that old covenant promises to ethnic Israel remain in effect when the New Testament explicitly says the covenant is obsolete. This isn't subtle or ambiguous—the language is clear and direct. Those who teach that God must still fulfill land promises, restore the Levitical priesthood, or establish a millennial Jewish kingdom are, in Shamoun's view, contradicting the plain teaching of the New Testament. The argument advances by showing that this obsolescence isn't arbitrary but is grounded in Israel's failure, predicted by the prophets, and confirmed by both Scripture and history.

Practical Implications: Understanding that the old covenant is obsolete has massive implications for how Christians read both Testaments. It means Old Testament laws, rituals, and national promises to Israel must be filtered through the New Testament's interpretation. Some aspects find direct fulfillment in Christ (sacrificial system → Christ's sacrifice; priesthood → Christ's priesthood; temple → Christ's body and the Church). Some are universalized (blessings promised to Israel → blessings for all in Christ). Some are simply ended (dietary laws, purity codes, Levitical rituals). This affects practical issues: Christians don't offer animal sacrifices, observe Levitical purity laws, or make pilgrimage to Jerusalem, because the covenant that required these things is obsolete. It also affects eschatology: if the old covenant is obsolete, then expecting God to restore its institutions (rebuilt temple, renewed Levitical priesthood, political kingdom for ethnic Israel) contradicts the New Testament's teaching. The practical application extends to apologetics: when Seventh Day Adventists argue Christians must observe the Sabbath, or when Hebrew Roots movements insist on Torah observance, the answer is that these requirements belonged to the now-obsolete covenant. When Christian Zionists argue political support for Israel is biblical mandate, the answer is that the national-territorial promises belonged to the obsolete covenant and aren't affirmed in the new.

Analogy: Imagine a technology company that developed a revolutionary operating system (OS 1.0) in its early years. This OS served its purpose, allowing the company to establish itself, serve customers, and develop expertise. But as technology advanced, the company developed a far superior operating system (OS 2.0) that was faster, more secure, more user-friendly, and had vastly expanded capabilities. The company announced that on a certain date, OS 1.0 would be sunset—it would no longer receive updates, support would end, and users needed to migrate to OS 2.0. This doesn't mean OS 1.0 was bad or failed; it accomplished exactly what it was designed to do in its time. But its time has passed. Now imagine some customers insisting the company must continue supporting OS 1.0, arguing, "But you promised this system would meet our computing needs!" The company would respond, "It did meet your needs, for its time. But we've now provided something far better. Your needs are met more fully in OS 2.0." Persisting with OS 1.0 after OS 2.0 is available isn't faithfulness but foolishness. Similarly, the old covenant accomplished its purposes beautifully—it revealed sin, preserved the messianic line, provided types of Christ, prepared the world for the gospel. But now that Christ has come and established the new covenant, attempting to maintain the old covenant system (or expecting God to restore it) misunderstands both covenants' purposes.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: The Distinction Between God's Fault and Human Fault

Hebrews 8:7-8 makes a crucial distinction that prevents misunderstanding: "For if that first covenant had been faultless, there would have been no occasion sought for a second. For finding fault with them, he says..." The fault was not inherent in the covenant itself (which was "holy, righteous, and good" according to Romans 7:12) but in the people who received it. They couldn't and wouldn't keep its terms. This matters because it shows God's character remains consistent—He didn't arbitrarily scrap a good system, nor did He give Israel a defective covenant. Rather, He gave them a covenant appropriate to that stage of redemptive history, knowing (in His omniscience) it would reveal their inability to save themselves and their need for a Savior. The new covenant addresses the human fault by providing what the old couldn't: regeneration through the Spirit, the law written on hearts rather than tablets, genuine forgiveness rather than temporary covering, and Christ's perfect righteousness imputed to believers. Shamoun's emphasis on this distinction prevents the anti-Semitic distortion that God simply gave up on Jews because they're inherently flawed. No—all humanity is flawed (Romans 3:23), and the old covenant was designed to reveal that universal problem so that the universal solution (Christ) would be welcomed.

Sub-point B: Historical Confirmation Through Temple Destruction

When Shamoun mentions the Levitical priesthood has "disappeared for over 1,900 years now" and notes that when Hebrews was written the temple "had not yet been destroyed," he's drawing attention to remarkable prophetic fulfillment. Hebrews likely was written between 64-68 AD (before Jerusalem's destruction but during early persecution). The author says the old covenant is "ready to vanish away"—present tense, imminent expectation. Within a few years (70 AD), Roman armies under Titus besieged Jerusalem, destroyed the temple, slaughtered over a million Jews, and ended the sacrificial system permanently. This wasn't coincidental timing but divine confirmation. God visibly demonstrated the obsolescence of the old covenant by making it impossible to continue. There is no Levitical priesthood today because there's no functioning temple, no sacrificial system, no way to observe major elements of the Mosaic covenant. This has been true for 1,950+ years. Shamoun's point is devastating to Christian Zionism: if God intended to restore the old covenant system, why has He prevented its operation for two millennia? The historical fact of the priesthood's disappearance confirms the theological claim of the covenant's obsolescence.
