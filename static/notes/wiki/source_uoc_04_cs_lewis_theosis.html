Summary

A catechetical video from Ancient Faith Explained that meets the Protestant inquirer on
his own ground: it affirms, in full, the Protestant doctrine of sanctification (past,
present, and future tenses) and then argues that Orthodoxy says something more — that
salvation is theosis, real participation in the divine life. Its hook is that the most
widely read Protestant author of the 20th century, C.S. Lewis, already taught this openly
in Mere Christianity ("little Christs," "gods and goddesses") without anyone calling him a
heretic. The video's center of gravity is the one safeguard that keeps theosis from becoming
blasphemy: St. Gregory Palamas's distinction between God's unknowable essence (ousia)
and His genuinely-God, participable energies (energeiai) — illustrated by the sun whose
essence would destroy you but whose light and warmth you partake of daily. It closes by
naming the three precise points where theosis exceeds Protestant sanctification — scope
(ontological, not merely moral), ceiling (no end, even in eternity), and medium (the
body is deified too, through the Eucharist, fasting, and the cross).

Key Points

  • 2 Peter 1:4 read literally — "partakers of the divine nature" (theoi koinonoi physeos)
    is the biblical seed; "most Protestants skip past this verse" but it "should shake the
    foundations of how you understand salvation itself."
  • Protestants are right about sanctification — the video affirms all three tenses:
    you were sanctified (1 Cor 6:11), you are being sanctified (2 Cor 3:18), you will be
    sanctified/glorified (1 John 3:2). Westminster, Heidelberg, Calvin, Luther, Wesley all
    teach real Spirit-worked transformation, completed at the resurrection. "All of this the
    Orthodox Church affirms, every single word of it."
  • Theosis = becoming God by grace, never by nature — "We never become the creator." The
    distance between God and humanity is bridged "not merely by forgiveness, but by real union."
  • It is apostolic, not medieval — Irenaeus (~180 AD, disciple of Polycarp, disciple of
    John): "the word of God became what we are, so that he might bring us to be what he is
    himself." Athanasius: "God became man so that man might become God." Gregory of Nazianzus:
    "let us become gods for his sake, since he became man for ours."
  • Psalm 82:6 / John 10 — "You are gods, and all of you are sons of the Most High" is the
    verse Jesus Himself quoted (John 10:34) to defend His divinity; it is the OT root of the
    deification language.
  • The Palamite safeguard — without the essence/energies distinction, theosis "would be
    heresy." God's essence (ousia) is unknowable and unapproachable; His energies
    (energeiai) — His love, light, glory, grace — "are God himself extended toward creation,"
    uncreated, and in these we genuinely participate.
  • The sun analogy — you cannot touch the sun (its essence would destroy you), yet every
    day you partake of it: heat, light, growth. "You partake of the sun without becoming the
    sun. Theosis is exactly that."
  • Three points of divergence from Protestant sanctification:
    1. Scope — sanctification is framed morally/ethically (becoming better behaved);
      theosis is ontological (transfigured "in your very being").
    2. Ceiling — sanctification is capped at glorification, then "the story ends"; theosis
      "has no ceiling… you never stop becoming more like God," forever.
    3. Medium — sanctification is usually internal (Spirit on heart and mind); theosis is
      also bodily — "the Eucharist transforms your flesh. Fasting disciplines your flesh.
      The sign of the cross traces divine life onto your flesh."
  • The iron-in-fire image (recurring) — an iron sword in fire glows, radiates heat, shines,
    takes on the properties of flame, "but it never stops being iron." Participation, not
    identity — "the iron really does become fire by participation, and the fire is never
    exhausted."
  • C.S. Lewis was not speaking metaphorically — in Mere Christianity the Church exists to
    turn people into "little Christs," "true and everlasting and really divine persons"; his
    house-into-palace image ("you thought you were being patched up… God is actually building a
    palace. He intends to live in it himself") is theosis "in 20th century English."
  • Lewis is not alone — the Wesleyan doctrine of entire sanctification approaches theosis,
    and the Finnish school of Lutheran theology argues Luther himself taught a form of
    theosis in his doctrine of union with Christ. "It is the doctrine every Protestant who reads
    the fathers eventually rediscovers."
  • The pastoral close — the dispute is not who is "more transformed." "Protestants are right
    that sanctification is real. Orthodox are right that theosis is more." The real question:
    "how high you dare to let transformation aim." "Holy things for the holy" at the chalice is
    spoken "to people in whom the iron is already becoming fire."

Details

Module: Thematic / Main Points Analysis

Section Overview
The video is a textbook irenic apologetic: rather than attacking Protestant soteriology, it
grants it in full and then extends it. The rhetorical spine is "yes, and more" — yes,
sanctification is real and biblical; and the Fathers, the Greek of 2 Peter, and even C.S.
Lewis point past sanctification to deification. Structurally it moves: the startling verse
(2 Pet 1:4) → what Protestants rightly hold (three tenses of sanctification) → the bolder
Orthodox claim (theosis, by grace) → its apostolic pedigree (Irenaeus, Athanasius, Gregory) →
the indispensable safeguard (essence/energies) → the three precise divergences → the C.S.
Lewis "plot twist" → a pastoral invitation. For a catechumen, its most useful feature is that
it refuses a zero-sum frame: it lets you keep everything true in your Protestant formation
while showing where that formation stopped short.

Main Point 1 — 2 Peter 1:4 is the seed Protestants skip

  • Core Argument: "Through God's promises we may become… theoi koinonoi physeos, partakers
    of the divine nature." Taken literally it "says something that should shake the foundations
    of how you understand salvation itself," yet "most Protestants skip past this verse without
    slowing down."
  • Biblical Foundation: 2 Peter 1:4; reinforced by Psalm 82:6 and Christ's use of it in
    John 10:34.
  • Argument Development: The whole video is, in effect, a slow reading of this one verse —
    every later move (the Fathers, the energies, the three divergences) is an answer to "what
    could partaker of the divine nature actually mean without becoming blasphemy?"
  • Practical Implications: Invites the inquirer to stop reading Scripture through the ceiling
    of his tradition's categories and let an "uncomfortable" verse reset the size of salvation.
  • Analogy: The narrator's framing — a verse "skipped past" like a door painted the same
    color as the wall; once you see it, you cannot unsee how much room is on the other side.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: St. Athanasius reads 2 Peter 1:4 as the very logic of the
      Incarnation — the Word assumed humanity precisely so humanity could be made "partaker," not
      as a figure of speech but as the goal of redemption.
    • Ascetic Movement: This verse names the telos of the whole ascetic ascent: katharsis →
      photismos → theosis. The "promises" are appropriated by purification, not merely affirmed.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: It is read at Vespers and sung in the Church's hymnody of
      the saints, who are venerated precisely as those in whom "partaker of the divine nature"
      became visible.

Main Point 2 — What Protestants are right about: the three tenses of sanctification

  • Core Argument: "Protestant Christianity has a beautiful doctrine of transformation."
    You were sanctified (set apart as holy in Christ at conversion), you are being sanctified
    (the Spirit progressively conforming you to Christ), you will be sanctified (completed at
    Christ's return). "All of this the Orthodox Church affirms, every single word of it."
  • Biblical Foundation: 1 Corinthians 6:11 ("you were washed… you were sanctified"); 2
    Corinthians 3:18 ("transformed from one degree of glory to another"); 1 John 3:2 ("we shall
    be like him, for we shall see him as he is").
  • Historical Context: Explicitly credits the Westminster Standards, the Heidelberg
    Catechism, and Calvin, Luther, and Wesley as teaching "real transformation of the believer
    worked by the Spirit, completed at the resurrection."
  • Argument Development: This is the apologetic's load-bearing concession — by affirming the
    Reformed and Wesleyan doctrine without caveat, the video earns the right to say "more"
    without being heard as "you have nothing."
  • Practical Implications: Frees the catechumen from the false anxiety that becoming Orthodox
    means repudiating the genuine sanctifying grace he has known.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: The Fathers, too, speak of salvation in tenses — "I have been
      saved, I am being saved, I shall be saved" — but they read the verbs as stages of
      participation in God, not only of forensic standing.
    • Ascetic Movement: The "present tense" (being sanctified) is the whole field of
      synergeia — the Spirit's grace met by the ascetic struggle of the will.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: Each Liturgy, confession, and fast is the "being
      sanctified" enacted; the saints and the resurrection are the "will be" already tasted.

Main Point 3 — The bolder claim: theosis, becoming God by grace

  • Core Argument: "The Orthodox Church calls salvation theosis, deification, or more
    literally becoming God." The immediate guardrail: "theosis does not mean we become God by
    nature. We never become the creator… Theosis means we become God by grace, that we genuinely
    participate in the divine life."
  • Historical Context: Presented as apostolic, not a later development — anchored to Irenaeus
    (~180 AD), two generations from the Apostle John through Polycarp.
  • Biblical Foundation: 2 Peter 1:4; Psalm 82:6; John 10:34.
  • Argument Development: The by-nature/by-grace distinction is the first of two safeguards
    (the second being essence/energies in Point 4). Together they make "becoming God"
    intelligible rather than pantheistic.
  • Practical Implications: Raises the entire horizon of the Christian life from moral
    betterment to union — reframing why the Church, the Mysteries, and asceticism exist at all.
  • Analogy: Adoption into a royal family — you genuinely become a son and share the family's
    life and honor without becoming the king who begot the heir by nature.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: Athanasius and Gregory of Nazianzus state it most boldly ("God
      became man so that man might become God"); the boldness is deliberate and dogmatically safe
      because of the by-grace qualifier.
    • Ascetic Movement: Theosis is the explicit goal of hesychasm and the Philokalic
      tradition — not extraordinary experience sought for its own sake, but union pursued through
      humility.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The deified saints (and their incorrupt relics, their
      luminous icons) are the Church's living evidence that "by grace" is not a metaphor.

Main Point 4 — The indispensable safeguard: essence and energies

  • Core Argument: In the 14th century Barlaam of Calabria mocked the Athonite monks' claim to
    experience the uncreated light, insisting "God's essence is unknowable. You cannot experience
    God directly." Palamas answered with the distinction: God's essence (ousia) is "what
    God is in himself, unknowable, unapproachable" — to touch it we "would either cease to exist
    or become God ourselves" — but God's energies (energeiai) — "his love, his light, his
    glory, his grace" — "are not created effects. They are God himself extended toward creation,
    and in these we can genuinely participate." "Without it, you cannot explain how partakers of
    the divine nature can be anything other than blasphemy."
  • Historical Context: The Palamite controversy (14th c.); dogmatized at the Constantinople
    councils of 1341 and 1351 against Barlaam's denial. (See concept_palamism_and_divine_energies.)
  • Biblical Foundation: Coheres with the NT vocabulary of energeia (God's active working,
    Eph 1:19, 3:7; Col 1:29) and the theophanies (burning bush, Sinai, the Transfiguration) as
    uncreated self-manifestations.
  • Practical Implications: It is the single doctrine that lets a former Protestant hold
    "real union with God" and "God remains wholly transcendent" at the same time — neither
    pantheism nor mere imitation.
  • Analogy: The narrator's sun — you cannot touch the sun (its essence would destroy you),
    yet you live every day by partaking of its light, heat, and energy. (This is the same logic
    as the "bare electric wire" analogy on concept_theosis.)
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: St. Basil's Letter 234 — "we know God from His energies, but we
      do not approach near His essence" — is the patristic seed Palamas systematized.
    • Ascetic Movement: The energies are what the purified nous perceives as the uncreated
      light (photismos); the safeguard also guards against prelest — mistaking imagination for
      the energies of God.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The Transfiguration feast and the hesychast prayer of the
      heart are where the energies/essence theology is lived, not merely argued.

Main Point 5 — Where theosis exceeds sanctification: scope, ceiling, medium

  • Core Argument: Three precise divergences.
    (1) Scope — Protestant sanctification is "usually described in moral and ethical terms…
    you are becoming a better person," whereas theosis "is ontological. You are not just becoming
    better behaved, you are being transfigured in your very being."
    (2) Ceiling — sanctification is "typically capped at glorification… and then the story
    ends," whereas "theosis has no ceiling. Even in eternity, you never stop becoming more like
    God. There is infinite divine life to be partaken of, and you will partake of it forever."
    (3) Medium — sanctification is "usually internal, a work of the spirit on the heart and
    mind," whereas "in Orthodoxy, the body is part of theosis. The Eucharist transforms your
    flesh. Fasting disciplines your flesh. The sign of the cross traces divine life onto your
    flesh. The whole person is being deified, including the matter you are made of."
  • Biblical Foundation: The ontological reading rests on 2 Pet 1:4 and 2 Cor 3:18 ("from
    glory to glory" with no terminus stated); the bodily medium on the Eucharistic and incarnational
    logic ("the body is for the Lord," 1 Cor 6:13ff., the chapter that also gives "you were
    sanctified").
  • Practical Implications: Names exactly what the catechumen gains in the Orthodox frame:
    not a contradiction of his sanctification, but its deepening (being, not just behavior), its
    un-capping (eternal ascent), and its embodiment (fasting, communion, the cross as participation).
  • Analogy: The iron-in-fire image returns here — the iron's transformation is in its being
    (it really radiates), without ceiling (the fire is "never exhausted"), and bodily (the iron
    itself, not an idea of iron, is changed).
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: St. Maximus the Confessor frames theosis as the whole creation,
      body included, gathered into God; St. Irenaeus insists "the flesh is saved."
    • Ascetic Movement: The "medium" point is the dogmatic basis for bodily asceticism —
      prostrations, fasting, vigils — as genuine participation, not mere discipline of the mind.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The Eucharist as "medicine of immortality," the bodily
      fast of the Church year, and the sign of the cross are the three "media" the video names,
      lived weekly.

Main Point 6 — The plot twist: C.S. Lewis (and Wesley, and the Finnish Luther)

  • Core Argument: Lewis "wasn't speaking metaphorically." Having read Athanasius and the
    Fathers, he wrote in Mere Christianity that the Church exists "to turn people into little
    Christs," that the whole purpose of Christianity is to make us "gods and goddesses," "true
    and everlasting and really divine persons" — using the image of a house being rebuilt into a
    palace God "intends to live in… himself." "Lewis taught it openly, and no one called him a
    heretic." Nor is he alone: the Wesleyan doctrine of entire sanctification "approaches
    theosis," and the Finnish school of Lutheran scholarship "argues that Luther himself
    taught a form of theosis in his doctrine of union with Christ."
  • Background Significance: Reframes theosis as "not a foreign doctrine smuggled in from the
    East" but "the doctrine every Protestant who reads the fathers eventually rediscovers" — a
    powerful move for an inquirer worried he is abandoning his heritage.
  • Practical Implications: Gives the catechumen a familiar, trusted bridge-figure (Lewis)
    and dissolves the "East vs. West" framing into "shallow vs. deep readings of the same gospel."
  • Analogy: Lewis's own — you came to God expecting to be "patched up" into a better cottage;
    He is building a palace and means to dwell in it.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: Lewis is, in effect, transmitting Athanasius; the video models how
      to read a beloved Western author back into the patristic stream he was drawing from.
    • Ascetic Movement: The "palace" is built, in Orthodox terms, by synergy — grace and
      ascetic cooperation — not by passive patching; Lewis's image needs the praxis to be complete.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The catechumen can keep Lewis on the shelf and read him as
      a doorway to the Liturgy, where "holy things for the holy" is the palace being inhabited.

Module: Bible Verse Deep Dive

Verse 1 — 2 Peter 1:4 (OSB/NKJV)

  • Text: "…that through these you may be partakers of the divine nature, having escaped the
    corruption that is in the world through lust." (Greek: theias koinonoi physeos.)
  • Speaker's Application: The thesis verse — read literally, salvation is participation in
    the divine nature    , the very definition of theosis; "most Protestants skip past" it.
  • Theological Significance: This is the explicit biblical warrant for deification; the entire
    Palamite framework exists to explain how this participation is real without confusing
    creature and Creator.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: Athanasius and Cyril treat it as the goal of the Incarnation; the
      "promises" are the means by which the corruptible puts on incorruption.
    • Ascetic Movement: "Having escaped the corruption… through lust" makes katharsis (the
      healing of the passions) the precondition of participation — theosis is not bypassed asceticism.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: Appointed in the Church's lectionary and echoed in the
      hymns of the saints, who embody "partaker of the divine nature."

Verse 2 — Psalm 82:6 / John 10:34 (OSB/NKJV)

  • Text: "I said, 'You are gods, and all of you are children of the Most High'" (Ps 82:6);
    "Jesus answered them, 'Is it not written in your law, "I said, you are gods"?'" (John 10:34).
  • Speaker's Application: The OT root of deification language — and Jesus Himself wields it
    (John 10) in defense of His own divinity, legitimizing the "you are gods" vocabulary.
  • Theological Significance: Shows the bold "becoming gods" speech of Athanasius and Gregory
    is not patristic excess but a citation of Scripture's own idiom, ratified by Christ.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: The Fathers read "you are gods" as deification by grace/adoption
      (huiothesia) — sons of the Most High, not rivals to Him.
    • Ascetic Movement: The verse pairs divine sonship with judgment in its psalm ("yet you
      shall die like men") — deification is a vocation that can be refused, keeping the ascetic
      stakes real.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: Psalm 81 (LXX) is read in the Church; its "arise, O God,
      judge the earth" is sung at Holy Saturday, the threshold of the new creation in which the
      "gods by grace" are raised.

Verse 3 — 1 Corinthians 6:11 (OSB/NKJV) — the "past tense"

  • Text: "And such were some of you. But you were washed, but you were sanctified, but you
    were justified in the name of the Lord Jesus and by the Spirit of our God."
  • Speaker's Application: Establishes you were sanctified — set apart as holy at conversion;
    the foundation the video grants to Protestant soteriology in full.
  • Theological Significance: The Orthodox affirm this initial sanctification but read it as
    the beginning of participation (baptismal regeneration), not its completion.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: "You were washed" is baptism; Chrysostom ties the aorist verbs to
      the font as the inauguration of the new life.
    • Ascetic Movement: The same chapter ("your body is the temple of the Holy Spirit," 6:19)
      grounds the bodily medium of theosis the video names in Point 5.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: Baptism and Chrismation enact "you were washed… sanctified"
      — the catechumen's own coming reception.

Verse 4 — 2 Corinthians 3:18 (OSB/NKJV) — the "present tense"

  • Text: "But we all, with unveiled face, beholding as in a mirror the glory of the Lord, are
    being transformed into the same image from glory to glory, just as by the Spirit of the Lord."
  • Speaker's Application: Establishes you are being sanctified — and, crucially, "from
    glory to glory" with no stated terminus, which the video uses for the "no ceiling" point.
  • Theological Significance: Metamorphoumetha ("being transformed," same verb as the
    Transfiguration) makes ongoing sanctification a genuine metamorphosis — the ontological
    reading, not mere moral improvement.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: The Fathers read "from glory to glory" as the endless ascent
      (Gregory of Nyssa's epektasis) — the soul's perpetual progress into God.
    • Ascetic Movement: "Beholding… the glory" is theoria; the verse maps the photismos →
      theosis movement of the purified nous.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The unveiled-face beholding is realized in the Liturgy and
      in the prayer of the heart, where the glory is contemplated.

Verse 5 — 1 John 3:2 (OSB/NKJV) — the "future tense"

  • Text: "Beloved, now we are children of God; and it has not yet been revealed what we shall
    be, but we know that when He is revealed, we shall be like Him, for we shall see Him as He is."
  • Speaker's Application: Establishes you will be glorified — the future completion both
    traditions confess; the video then asks whether even this is a ceiling or a threshold.
  • Theological Significance: "We shall be like Him, for we shall see Him as He is" is the
    beatific-vision/theoria hope; for the Orthodox the seeing is the transforming (we become
    like Him by seeing Him), and it has no end.
  • Orthodox Theological Lens:
    • The Father's Reading: The Fathers read "see Him as He is" through the energies — we see
      and are deified by the uncreated light, never the unapproachable essence.
    • Ascetic Movement: "Everyone who has this hope purifies himself" (the very next verse,
      3:3) — the future vision drives present katharsis.
    • Orthodox Practice Connection: The eschatological "we shall be like Him" is the content of
      the Church's hope sung at every Paschal and funeral service.

Orthodox Synthesis

Orthodox Reading of This Video:

For you, a catechumen coming out of a Protestant world, this video is almost ideally pitched:
it does not ask you to throw away the sanctification you were taught — it asks you to follow it
all the way up. Everything it grants to the Reformers and to Wesley is genuinely affirmed by
the Church; the only quarrel is with a ceiling. The decisive thing to carry away is not the
C.S. Lewis "gotcha" (delightful as it is) but the essence/energies safeguard: theosis is
glorious and orthodox only because we participate in God's uncreated energies, never His
essence — which is exactly why "partakers of the divine nature" is gospel and not blasphemy.
Hold the three divergences as a checklist for your own heart: am I letting salvation be merely
moral (better behavior) when it is meant to be ontological (a changed being)? Am I quietly
capping it at "going to heaven" when the Fathers promise an ascent with no end? Am I treating
my faith as something purely internal when the Church hands me bodily means — the Eucharist,
the fast, the cross traced on my own flesh — to be deified as a whole person? This video pairs
directly with the afterlife video before it: there, salvation was "being made able to bear the
light"; here, that capacity is named — theosis, the iron becoming fire.

Ascetic posture: Receive every fast, prostration, and Communion not as moral self-improvement
but as your flesh itself being drawn into the fire; and when the old Protestant instinct says
"this is too much, too high," answer with 2 Peter 1:4 — the height is the gospel's own.

Key Concept Highlights

  • Theosis by grace, not by nature — real union, never becoming the Creator.
  • Essence (ousia) vs. energies (energeiai) — the Palamite safeguard that makes
    deification orthodox; the sun analogy.
  • Three tenses of sanctification — were / are being / will be (1 Cor 6:11; 2 Cor 3:18;
    1 John 3:2) — fully affirmed, then extended.
  • Scope / ceiling / medium — ontological not merely moral; endless not capped; bodily not
    only internal.
  • The C.S. Lewis bridge — "little Christs," "gods and goddesses"; Wesleyan entire
    sanctification and the Finnish-school Luther as Western rediscoveries of theosis.

Section Summary
The video's strength is its generosity: by affirming Protestant sanctification without reserve
and then producing C.S. Lewis as a witness against the inquirer's own suspicion, it makes
theosis feel less like conversion to a foreign doctrine and more like the completion of one
already half-believed. Its theological center is the essence/energies distinction, without which
the whole edifice ("becoming God") collapses into pantheism or blasphemy; its practical center
is the three divergences, which tell the catechumen precisely what the Orthodox frame adds —
depth of being, endlessness, and the body. The iron-in-fire image binds the two videos of the
series so far: salvation is learning to bear the fire (video 1), and that bearing is
deification (video 4).
