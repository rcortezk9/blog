The TRUE Meaning of "My God, My God, Why Have You Forsaken Me?" - Complete Analysis

Video Information

  • Speaker: Sam Shamoun
  • Channel: Faith With Reason
  • Duration: 10 minutes
  • Date: October 19, 2025
  • Primary Scripture: Psalm 22

Section Overview

This teaching represents a masterclass in contextual biblical interpretation, addressing one of the most misunderstood moments in Scripture: Jesus' cry from the cross, "My God, My God, why have you forsaken me?" Sam Shamoun systematically dismantles the popular misconception that these words express despair or divine abandonment, demonstrating instead that they constitute a deliberate quotation of Psalm 22—a prophetic psalm of deliverance and vindication. The presentation unfolds in three strategic movements: first, establishing the proper interpretation of Psalm 22 by examining its full context rather than isolated verses; second, demonstrating how Jesus' quotation signals the moment of His deliverance rather than abandonment; and third, addressing sophisticated theological objections about how Jesus can simultaneously be God while having a God over Him.

The teaching's brilliance lies in its multi-layered apologetic approach. Shamoun doesn't merely correct a misunderstanding; he transforms a supposed theological problem into powerful evidence for orthodox Christology and Trinitarian theology. By weaving together Psalm 22, Isaiah 53, Jeremiah 32:27, and John 1:14, he constructs an interpretive framework that simultaneously explains Jesus' words on the cross, defends the doctrine of the Incarnation, and refutes anti-Trinitarian objections. The presentation moves from textual analysis to theological synthesis, showing how proper biblical interpretation naturally leads to orthodox Christian doctrine.

This section functions as both defensive and offensive apologetics—defensive in correcting misinterpretations that undermine Christ's deity and the Father-Son relationship, offensive in demonstrating how these very texts, properly understood, establish rather than challenge traditional Christian theology. The teaching's significance extends beyond academic interest; it touches the heart of Christian faith by clarifying what was actually happening at history's most crucial moment—when Christ secured our redemption through His sacrificial death.

Detailed Point Analysis

Main Point 1: Psalm 22 is a Psalm of Deliverance, Not Despair

Core Argument: The fundamental error in interpreting Jesus' cry lies in reading Psalm 22:1 in isolation rather than in its complete literary and theological context. When the entire psalm is examined, it becomes unmistakably clear that this is not an expression of hopelessness or abandonment but rather a confident prayer for deliverance with the expectation of divine response. Shamoun demonstrates that the opening cry "My God, My God, why have you forsaken me?" must be understood in light of the immediate follow-up in verse 1b: "Why are you so far from helping me and from the words of my roaring?" This parallel structure reveals that "forsaken" doesn't mean "permanently abandoned" but rather "why is deliverance delayed?" The psalmist is not doubting God's presence or faithfulness; he is urgently pleading for God to act swiftly in his crisis. This interpretation completely transforms our understanding of Jesus' words on the cross.

Historical Context: In ancient Jewish worship and Scripture memorization, quotations served as shorthand references to entire passages. When a rabbi or teacher cited the opening line of a psalm or prophetic text, the educated listener would immediately recall the full context and development of that passage. This interpretive key is crucial for understanding Jesus' intention. By the first century, Psalm 22 was widely recognized as a messianic psalm describing the suffering and vindication of the righteous one. Jewish audiences hearing Jesus quote its opening words would naturally think of the psalm's progression from suffering to triumph, from pleading to praise. The darkness that had covered the land for three hours (mentioned in the Synoptic Gospels) provided the dramatic backdrop for this cry, and its subsequent lifting served as God's unmistakable answer to His Son's prayer.

Biblical Foundation: The scriptural principle at work here is the unity and coherence of biblical texts. Scripture interprets Scripture, and no verse can be properly understood in isolation from its immediate and broader context. Psalm 22 itself provides its own interpretive framework through its literary structure: verses 1-21 present the sufferer's crisis and appeal, while verses 22-31 celebrate God's deliverance and the global impact of His salvation. This movement from lament to praise is characteristic of the lament psalms, which consistently express confidence in God's ultimate faithfulness even while articulating present distress. The psalm teaches that crying out "why have you forsaken me?" is not incompatible with faith; rather, it's the cry of one who knows God well enough to expect His intervention.

Argument Development: This point establishes the hermeneutical foundation for everything that follows in Shamoun's presentation. By demonstrating that Psalm 22 is fundamentally about deliverance rather than abandonment, he sets up the logical framework for understanding Jesus' quotation as a declaration of imminent victory rather than an expression of defeat. This interpretation also connects to the broader biblical theme of the righteous sufferer who trusts God even in the darkest circumstances, a theme that runs from Job through the Psalms to Isaiah's Suffering Servant and ultimately finds its fulfillment in Christ. The teaching advances the thesis that Jesus was not experiencing a crisis of faith but was deliberately identifying Himself as the fulfillment of this prophetic psalm at the precise moment when His suffering was about to transition into triumph.

Practical Implications: This understanding profoundly affects how Christians interpret and respond to their own experiences of suffering. If even Jesus' most desperate cry was not actually an expression of divine abandonment but a confident prayer for deliverance, then believers can be assured that their own feelings of God's distance don't indicate His actual absence or unfaithfulness. The teaching models how to interpret emotional experiences through the lens of scriptural truth rather than allowing subjective feelings to override objective theological reality. It also demonstrates the importance of knowing Scripture thoroughly enough to understand quotations and allusions in their full context—a principle applicable to interpreting both the Bible itself and contemporary theological discussions.

Analogy: Consider a person watching a movie who walks into the theater during a dark, tense scene where the protagonist appears trapped and defeated. Without seeing what came before or staying to see what comes after, that viewer might conclude the movie is a tragedy about failure and despair. But those who watch the complete film understand that this dark moment is actually the dramatic low point before the hero's ultimate triumph—the darkness makes the coming victory all the more glorious. Similarly, reading Psalm 22:1 without its full context is like judging an entire movie based on its most intense crisis scene. The full psalm reveals that the "forsaken" cry is actually part of a larger narrative arc that moves inexorably toward deliverance, vindication, and worldwide worship of the God who saves.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: The Second Half of Verse 1 Clarifies the First Half
The key to proper interpretation lies in the parallelism within Psalm 22:1 itself. "Why have you forsaken me?" is immediately explained by "Why are you so far from helping me and from the words of my roaring?" This parallel structure, characteristic of Hebrew poetry, shows that "forsaken" and "far from helping" are synonymous expressions. The psalmist isn't questioning God's existence, presence, or covenant faithfulness—he's questioning the timing of God's intervention. It's similar to how a child might cry "Where are you, Mom?" not because they believe their mother has permanently abandoned them, but because they urgently need her help right now and wonder why she isn't immediately appearing. The cry expresses urgency and longing for help, not doubt about whether help will ultimately come. This interpretation is reinforced by the continued prayer throughout verses 2-21, where the psalmist repeatedly appeals to God's character, past faithfulness, and covenant promises.

Sub-point B: Verses 23-24 Reveal God's Immediate Response
The transition from crying out for deliverance (verses 1-21) to praising God for deliverance (verses 22-31) happens without any narrative gap or time indicator between them, suggesting immediate divine response. Shamoun emphasizes that verse 24 explicitly states: "He has not despised nor abhorred the affliction of the afflicted; neither has he hidden his face from him; but when he cried unto him, he heard." This verse directly answers and refutes any interpretation that sees Psalm 22 as describing actual divine abandonment. The Father did not hide His face; He did not despise the affliction; He heard the cry and responded. Applied to Jesus' situation, this means that when Jesus quoted Psalm 22:1 from the cross, God's immediate response (signified by removing the darkness) fulfilled the psalm's promise of deliverance. The cry was not the end of the story but the prelude to answered prayer and accomplished redemption.

Main Point 2: The Darkness Being Lifted Was God's Sign of Deliverance

Core Argument: Jesus' quotation of Psalm 22:1 functioned as both prophecy fulfillment and prayer, and the immediate removal of the supernatural darkness that had covered the land served as God the Father's visible, public response to His Son's cry. Shamoun establishes a direct cause-and-effect relationship: Jesus cried out the words of Psalm 22 (announcing the moment of His deliverance), and God answered by removing the darkness (demonstrating that He had heard and was delivering). This interpretation transforms what many see as a moment of divine absence into actually being a moment of dramatic divine presence and intervention. The darkness itself was not a sign of God abandoning Jesus but rather a sign of God's judgment being poured out on sin as Jesus bore humanity's iniquity. Its removal signaled that this judgment was complete and the work of redemption was accomplished.

Historical Context: The three hours of darkness (from the sixth to the ninth hour, approximately noon to 3 PM) during Jesus' crucifixion was no natural phenomenon. This occurred during Passover, at the full moon, making a natural solar eclipse impossible. Early church fathers and ancient historians recognized this as a supernatural event of cosmic significance. In biblical symbolism, darkness consistently represents judgment, sin, death, and separation from God's favorable presence (though never from His omnipresence). The darkness at the crucifixion signified that Jesus was bearing the full weight of humanity's sin and experiencing the judgment that should have fallen on sinners. When this darkness lifted after Jesus' cry, it signaled that the judicial transaction was complete—sin's penalty had been fully paid. First-century Jews would have immediately recognized the theological significance of darkness and light as representing judgment and salvation, condemnation and vindication.

Biblical Foundation: Throughout Scripture, God's hearing and answering prayer is often accompanied by visible, physical signs. When Elijah prayed on Mount Carmel, fire fell from heaven (1 Kings 18). When the temple was dedicated, God's glory filled it visibly (2 Chronicles 7). When Jesus was baptized, the heavens opened and the Spirit descended visibly like a dove (Matthew 3). This pattern establishes that God often provides tangible, observable confirmations of His response to prayers at crucial moments in salvation history. The principle of divine signs authenticating divine actions runs throughout Scripture, and the crucifixion—history's most important moment—would naturally include such confirmation. The removal of darkness after Jesus' cry follows this established biblical pattern of God providing physical evidence of His spiritual action.

Argument Development: This point builds directly on the previous one by showing not just that Psalm 22 is about deliverance, but that this deliverance was visibly manifested at the crucifixion. Shamoun's argument creates an interpretive chain: (1) Psalm 22 promises that God will hear and deliver the righteous sufferer; (2) Jesus deliberately quoted this psalm to signal that the moment of deliverance had arrived; (3) God confirmed this by immediately removing the darkness; therefore (4) Jesus was not abandoned but delivered exactly as the psalm prophesied. This interpretation transforms the cross from a scene of divine absence to a scene of divine action—not God turning away from Jesus, but God actively receiving the sacrifice and signaling its acceptance. The teaching advances toward its theological climax by showing that what seemed like Jesus' darkest moment was actually His moment of triumph.

Practical Implications: This understanding teaches believers to look for God's responses to prayer, which may come in ways we don't initially recognize. Just as the disciples might have missed the significance of the darkness lifting if they didn't understand Psalm 22's context, Christians today might miss God's answers to their prayers if they don't understand biblical patterns and principles. It also demonstrates that God's greatest acts of deliverance often come at the moment of deepest crisis, not before it. Jesus wasn't delivered from the cross but through the cross, just as God's people throughout Scripture are often delivered through trials rather than from them. This challenges the prosperity gospel notion that God's favor always means immediate escape from difficulty; instead, it shows that God's deliverance often comes through the valley of the shadow of death rather than around it.

Analogy: Imagine a skilled surgeon performing a critical operation. There's a point in the procedure where the patient's vital signs look most alarming, where things seem most dangerous—but this is actually the exact moment when the surgeon is removing the life-threatening tumor. To an untrained observer monitoring only the patient's vital signs without understanding the procedure, this might look like the moment the patient is closest to death. But to the surgeon and informed observers, this is actually the moment of healing and deliverance. The temporary crisis is necessary for the permanent cure. Similarly, Jesus' cry and the darkness at the crucifixion might look like divine abandonment to those who don't understand what God was actually accomplishing. But to those who understand the "surgical procedure" of redemption that God was performing, the darkness and its subsequent removal signal successful completion of the operation that brings eternal healing.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: The Darkness Represented God's Judgment on Sin, Not Absence from Jesus
The supernatural darkness should not be interpreted as God the Father abandoning God the Son, but rather as visible evidence that God's judgment against sin was being poured out on Jesus as He bore humanity's iniquity. Isaiah 53:10 states that "it pleased the LORD to bruise him; he has put him to grief." This indicates the Father's active role in the crucifixion—not turning away from Jesus, but actively treating Him as the sin-bearer for humanity's redemption. The darkness manifested what was happening spiritually: Jesus was experiencing the full weight of God's wrath against sin. But this was covenant faithfulness, not abandonment; it was the Father and Son working in perfect unity to accomplish salvation, not the Father rejecting the Son. The darkness didn't represent divine absence but divine action—God was present and active in judging sin at the cross.

Sub-point B: The Timing of the Darkness's Removal Confirms Shamoun's Interpretation
The gospels indicate that the darkness lifted after Jesus cried out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" but before He said "It is finished" (tetelestai) and "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit." This chronological sequence supports the interpretation that Jesus' cry signaled the completion of God's judgment and the arrival of deliverance. If the cry expressed hopeless abandonment, we would expect the darkness to continue or even intensify. Instead, its removal immediately following the cry serves as God's "Yes!" to His Son's prayer from Psalm 22. The darkness ended precisely when Jesus indicated (through quoting Psalm 22) that the moment of deliverance had arrived. This timing is not coincidental but confirmatory—God's visible response validating Jesus' announcement that the psalm's prophecy of deliverance was being fulfilled at that very moment.

Main Point 3: Connection Between Psalm 22 and Isaiah 53 Regarding God's Face

Core Argument: Shamoun draws an essential theological connection between Psalm 22:24's statement that God "has not hidden his face from him" and Isaiah 53's description of the Suffering Servant as "one from whom we hid our faces." This parallel is not contradictory but complementary, revealing two different perspectives on the same reality. Psalm 22 emphasizes that God the Father did not turn His face away from Jesus; He remained present, attentive, and responsive to His Son. Isaiah 53, however, describes humanity's reaction to the Suffering Servant—how people despised, rejected, and turned their faces away from Him. The contrast is deliberate and instructive: humanity rejected the Messiah, but the Father did not. Humans hid their faces in disgust and contempt, but God kept His face attentively turned toward His Son. This distinction is crucial for maintaining both the unity of the Father and Son and the effectiveness of Christ's atoning sacrifice.

Historical Context: In ancient Near Eastern culture, to "hide one's face" from someone was an expression of rejection, displeasure, or abandonment. Conversely, God "making His face shine upon" someone (as in the Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6:24-26) signified favor, blessing, and presence. When Isaiah prophesied that the Suffering Servant would be "one from whom we hid our faces," he was describing the Messiah's rejection by His own people—exactly what happened during Jesus' trials and crucifixion, when even His closest disciples fled and Peter denied Him. The religious leaders mocked Him, the crowds called for His crucifixion, and Roman soldiers spat on Him and struck His face. This fulfilled Isaiah's prophecy about human rejection. But Psalm 22's declaration that God did not hide His face reveals the Father's continued favor and presence despite the Son's humiliation and suffering. This distinction would have been immediately clear to first-century Jewish readers familiar with both passages.

Biblical Foundation: The theological principle underlying this connection is that God's judgment on sin (which Jesus bore) is fundamentally different from God's rejection of Jesus as a person. Throughout Scripture, God demonstrates the ability to maintain covenant faithfulness to His people even while judging their sin. He judged Israel's rebellion yet never permanently rejected His covenant people. In the case of Christ, God was treating Jesus as the sin-bearer (judging the sin He carried) while never rejecting Jesus Himself (maintaining the Father-Son relationship intact). This distinction is essential to orthodox theology. If God actually abandoned or rejected Jesus at the cross, the Trinity would have been fractured, and we would have no assurance that God won't similarly abandon us. But if God remained faithful to Christ even while judging the sin Christ bore, we can be confident that "nothing can separate us from the love of God which is in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:38-39).

Argument Development: This point adds crucial nuance to the discussion by addressing a potential objection: "But didn't Isaiah say God's face was hidden from the Suffering Servant?" Shamoun's response demonstrates sophisticated biblical interpretation by showing that Isaiah and the psalmist are describing different actors' responses to the Messiah. This maintains the coherence of Scripture while deepening our understanding of what happened at the cross. The argument progression moves from establishing that Psalm 22 is about deliverance (Point 1) to showing that God actively delivered Jesus by removing the darkness (Point 2) to now defending this interpretation against scriptural passages that might seem to contradict it (Point 3). This anticipates and answers objections, strengthening the overall case.

Practical Implications: This teaching provides crucial comfort to believers experiencing rejection or suffering. Even when the entire world turns against us, God does not turn His face away from those who are in Christ. The distinction between divine discipline and divine rejection is vital for spiritual health. A believer may experience God's corrective discipline (Hebrews 12:5-11) or may face trials that conform us to Christ's image (Romans 8:28-29), but these experiences are radically different from being abandoned by God. The teaching also models how to harmonize biblical passages that initially seem contradictory—by careful attention to subject, perspective, and context. What appears to be a contradiction often reveals complementary truth when properly examined.

Analogy: Consider a loving parent who must take their child to receive painful but necessary medical treatment—perhaps a surgery or vaccination. The parent stays with the child throughout the ordeal, holding their hand, speaking words of comfort, never leaving their side. Yet the child experiences real pain and may even feel temporarily that the parent isn't protecting them from hurt. Meanwhile, other children in the waiting room might turn away, scared by the sick child's appearance or the medical procedures. Two very different reactions: the other children hide their faces in fear and rejection, but the parent keeps their face lovingly fixed on their child through all the pain, even though that parent is the one who brought the child to this place of necessary suffering. Similarly, humanity hid its face from Jesus in rejection (Isaiah 53), but the Father never turned His face away (Psalm 22:24). The Father kept His eyes on His Son through all the suffering He ordained for our redemption.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: Isaiah 59:1-2 Clarifies That Sin Creates Separation, Not Suffering
When the companion passage references Isaiah 59:1-2—"Your iniquities have separated between you and your God, and your sins have hid his face from you"—it establishes a crucial principle: sin causes separation from God, not suffering in itself. Jesus bore sin but never committed sin. The sins that separated humanity from God were laid on Jesus (Isaiah 53:6: "the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all"), but Jesus Himself remained personally sinless. Therefore, while Jesus experienced the judicial consequences of sin-bearing (judgment, suffering, death), He never experienced the relational consequences of sin (being rejected by the Father or separated from intimate fellowship). God's face was hidden from "us" (humanity) because of "our" sins, but God's face remained attentively turned toward His sinless Son even as that Son bore our sins. This distinction is essential for understanding how the incarnate Christ could simultaneously experience God's judgment of sin while maintaining unbroken communion with the Father.

Sub-point B: The Unity of the Godhead Means Internal Consistency Between Persons
The teaching implicitly affirms Trinitarian theology by showing that what one person of the Trinity says must cohere with what the other persons say and do. If Psalm 22 (spoken by Christ the Word even before His incarnation, as Shamoun will later demonstrate) declares that God doesn't hide His face from the afflicted one, this must align with the Father's actual treatment of the Son at the cross. The Father cannot contradict or fail to fulfill what the Son prophetically declared. This internal consistency within the Godhead guarantees that Psalm 22's promise of divine presence and deliverance was genuinely fulfilled at the crucifixion. The unity of purpose and action among Father, Son, and Spirit means that all three persons were actively involved in the crucifixion, working together in perfect harmony to accomplish redemption, not experiencing a fracture in their eternal relationship.

Main Point 4: The Incarnation Explains How Jesus Can Have a God

Core Argument: One of the most persistent objections to Christian theology, especially from Islamic and Unitarian critics, asks: "How can Jesus be God if He has a God over Him?" Shamoun provides a sophisticated answer rooted in Incarnational theology: Jesus didn't have a God over Him in His eternal divine nature, but the Father became "His God" specifically when the eternal Word became flesh. This distinction is not theological gymnastics but biblical precision. Shamoun demonstrates through John 1:14 that "the Word became flesh"—not the Father, not the Spirit, but specifically the Word. This act of incarnation meant that the second person of the Trinity took on human nature, becoming truly man while remaining truly God. In His human nature, Jesus properly relates to the Father as "His God" just as all humans relate to God. But in His divine nature, Jesus shares the same divine essence with the Father. The key is understanding that the incarnation added human nature to Christ's divine person without eliminating or diminishing His deity.

Historical Context: The Incarnation has been a central Christian doctrine since the apostolic age but has faced persistent challenges throughout church history. Early heresies either denied Christ's true humanity (Docetism, Gnosticism) or His true deity (Arianism, Adoptionism), or confused His two natures (Monophysitism, Monothelitism). The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) definitively articulated orthodox Christology: Jesus Christ is one person with two complete natures—fully God and fully man, united without confusion, change, separation, or division. Shamoun's teaching stands in this historic orthodox tradition, applying Chalcedonian Christology to explain how Jesus can simultaneously be God (in His divine nature) and have a God over Him (in His human nature). This understanding was developed to address exactly the kind of objection Shamoun is refuting, showing that the question "How can God have a God?" is based on a category confusion that fails to distinguish between Christ's two natures.

Biblical Foundation: The scriptural foundation for this teaching rests on several key passages. John 1:14 establishes that "the Word became flesh"—a specific divine person (the Word/Logos) took on human nature through the incarnation. Philippians 2:5-11 describes Jesus "being in very nature God" yet "taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness." Hebrews 2:14 states that "since the children have flesh and blood, he too shared in their humanity." These passages establish that Jesus' humanity is real, complete, and permanent (not merely a temporary appearance or illusion). Jeremiah 32:27, where God identifies Himself as "the God of all flesh," provides the crucial link: if God is the God of all flesh, and Jesus became flesh, then of course the Father would be Jesus' God in His incarnate state. This doesn't diminish Jesus' deity any more than a CEO becoming a frontline worker in his own company to understand his employees' experiences would mean he's no longer the CEO—he's holding both positions simultaneously.

Argument Development: This point represents a strategic shift in Shamoun's presentation from defending Scripture's teaching about the crucifixion to proactively addressing theological objections to Trinitarian Christology. The logic flows systematically: First, establish what the incarnation means (the Word became flesh). Second, show that God is "the God of all flesh" (Jeremiah 32:27). Third, demonstrate the logical conclusion: when the Word became flesh to serve the Father, the Father naturally became His God in that human capacity. This addresses the objection not by avoiding the difficulty but by embracing it and showing how it actually confirms orthodox theology. The teaching transforms what critics present as a problem (Jesus having a God) into evidence for the doctrine of the Incarnation. If Jesus didn't have the Father as His God after becoming incarnate, that would indicate His humanity wasn't genuine, contradicting Scripture's clear testimony that He was truly human.

Practical Implications: This theological clarity helps believers understand their own relationship with Christ and how to answer common objections to Christianity. It demonstrates that Jesus' example of submission to the Father doesn't contradict His deity but rather illustrates how the incarnate God-man perfectly fulfilled His mission. Christians can learn that submission and obedience don't imply inferiority—Jesus submitted to the Father in His human nature while being equal to the Father in His divine nature. This also provides a model for understanding how attributes can apply to a person in one capacity but not another. A judge in court possesses legal authority; the same person at home with their parents is a child who should honor father and mother. The same person can properly hold different relationships and roles simultaneously without contradiction. Similarly, Christ can be God over all creation in His divine nature while properly acknowledging the Father as His God in His human nature.

Analogy: Imagine a king who decides to experience life as an ordinary citizen in his kingdom to truly understand his people's needs and challenges. He doesn't cease being the king; he's not dethroned or demoted. But he voluntarily takes on the full experience of being a subject in his own kingdom—he faces the same laws, the same economic challenges, the same struggles as everyone else. In this capacity, he properly relates to the throne and the authority it represents, even though he himself is that very authority. He's simultaneously the king (in his essential identity) and a subject (in his voluntary, assumed experience). When he bows to the law of the land, he's not contradicting his kingship—he's demonstrating the perfection of his rule by showing that his laws are good even when they apply to himself. Similarly, when the eternal Word became flesh, He took on complete human experience, including the proper human relationship to God, without ceasing to be fully divine. When Jesus calls the Father "My God," He's speaking from His genuine human nature and experience, not denying His divine nature.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: Jeremiah 32:27 Proves God's Sovereignty Over All Flesh
The teaching emphasizes Jeremiah 32:27 where Jehovah declares, "I am the LORD, the God of all flesh. Is there anything too hard for me?" This establishes a universal principle: God is the God of all who possess flesh. This isn't about God being "over" flesh in a tyrannical sense, but about His covenant relationship, care, and authority over all humanity. The rhetorical question "Is there anything too hard for me?" connects to the incarnation's miracle—the impossible-sounding union of divine and human natures in one person. If nothing is too hard for God, then certainly He can accomplish the incarnation. When Jesus took on flesh, He entered into the category of "all flesh," and therefore the Father appropriately became "His God" in that capacity. This doesn't create a hierarchy within the Trinity's divine nature but recognizes the proper relationship between God and humanity—a relationship Jesus genuinely entered through the incarnation.

Sub-point B: Only the Word Became Flesh, Not the Father or Spirit
Shamoun carefully emphasizes that John 1:14 specifies "the Word became flesh"—not the Trinity as a whole, not the Father, not the Spirit, but specifically the second person. This is crucial because it means the incarnation affected only one person of the Trinity in terms of taking on human nature. The Father didn't become incarnate, so the Father never took on a relationship where another person was "His God." The Spirit didn't become incarnate in the same way. Only the Word/Son took on complete human nature, and therefore only the Son entered into the experience of having the Father as "His God." This preserves the distinct roles and missions of the three persons while maintaining their equality in divine essence. The economic Trinity (the roles and relationships among the persons in accomplishing salvation) is distinguished from the ontological Trinity (their essential being and equality). In the economic Trinity, the Son submits to the Father; in the ontological Trinity, they are co-equal and co-eternal.

Main Point 5: Psalm 22:10 Shows Precisely When the Father Became Jesus' God

Core Argument: The teaching reaches its climax with Shamoun's brilliant observation that Psalm 22:10 explicitly identifies exactly when the Father became Jesus' God: "I was cast upon you from the womb; you are my God from my mother's belly." This verse, spoken prophetically by Christ through David centuries before the incarnation, establishes that the Father-Son relationship, where the Father is Jesus' God, began specifically at Jesus' conception in Mary's womb. This timing is theologically precise and profoundly significant. Before the incarnation, the eternal Word existed in perfect equality with the Father, sharing the same divine essence without any relationship of dependency or hierarchy in being. But at the moment of conception—when the Word became flesh, when divine nature was united with human nature in the person of Jesus Christ—the Father assumed the role of "God" in relation to the Son's human nature. This isn't a change in the Trinity's essential relationships but an addition of a new dimension to the Son's experience through His genuine humanity.

Historical Context: The Jewish understanding of when human life begins is crucial to this point. Against modern abortion advocates who deny personhood to the unborn, Scripture consistently treats life as beginning at conception. Psalm 139:13-16 speaks of God's intimate involvement in forming the person in the womb. Luke 1:41 describes John the Baptist leaping in Elizabeth's womb when Mary (newly pregnant with Jesus) visited. Shamoun's teaching emphasizes that Psalm 22:10 says "from my mother's belly"—not "from my birth" but from conception. This means Jesus was fully human from the moment of conception in Mary's womb, not that humanity was added later at birth. The incarnation occurred at conception, and therefore the Father became "Jesus' God" from that precise moment. This has profound pro-life implications: if Jesus was fully human from conception, then all humans are persons from conception, deserving full protection and dignity.

Biblical Foundation: The theological principle demonstrated here is that Christ's humanity is as complete and genuine as His deity. If Jesus was truly "made in human likeness" (Philippians 2:7), "born of a woman" (Galatians 4:4), and "shared in our humanity" (Hebrews 2:14), then He must experience the full range of proper human relationships, including the relationship of creature to Creator, human to God. Every human properly relates to God as "my God" or "the LORD my God." This isn't an inferior position but the appropriate relationship of created to Creator, finite to Infinite. Jesus, in taking on complete humanity, enters fully into this relationship. Psalm 22:10 proves this relationship began at the incarnation's commencement (conception), not before. Prior to the incarnation, no such relationship existed because the Word existed only in His divine nature, as God not as human. The verse establishes both the beginning point (conception) and the reason (becoming human) for this relationship.

Argument Development: This final major point brings together all the previous elements into a comprehensive theological synthesis. Point 1 established that Psalm 22 is about deliverance. Point 2 showed that this deliverance was visibly confirmed at the cross. Point 3 harmonized Psalm 22 with Isaiah 53 regarding God's face. Point 4 explained how Jesus can be God while having a God through the doctrine of the Incarnation. Now Point 5 provides the temporal precision: exactly when did this relationship begin? At conception, when the Word became flesh. This completes the argument by showing that Jesus' words "My God, My God" on the cross aren't an expression of abandonment but a fulfillment of what Psalm 22 prophesied—that from the moment of His incarnation, the Father would be His God, and that this God would hear His cry and deliver Him. The teaching comes full circle: we began with Jesus' cry from the cross and end with understanding its root in His incarnational relationship with the Father.

Practical Implications: This teaching has multiple practical applications. First, it demonstrates the sanctity of life from conception—if Christ's humanity began at conception, all human life is sacred from that point. Second, it shows that having the Father as "His God" was not a humiliation for Jesus but the natural consequence of His mission to save humanity by becoming human. If Jesus embraced this relationship willingly for our sake, it can't be shameful or degrading. Third, it provides believers with confidence in prayer: if Jesus (who is God) prayed to the Father and was heard, how much more should we approach God confidently in prayer? Fourth, it models how to do biblical theology by tracing themes across multiple texts (Psalm 22, John 1, Jeremiah 32) and showing their internal consistency and mutual illumination.

Analogy: Think of an architect who designs a revolutionary new type of affordable housing for lower-income families. To truly understand whether his design is livable and meets real needs, he decides to live in one of these houses himself for a year, experiencing all the limitations and features as a resident would. Before moving in, he was the architect—the creator and authority over the project. But when he moves into the house, he becomes a resident, subject to the same building codes, the same utility costs, the same spatial constraints as everyone else who will live there. In this new capacity, he relates to building authority and housing standards as a subject, not as the creator. He hasn't stopped being the architect; his essential identity hasn't changed. But he's added a new dimension to his experience—he's now both architect and resident. The building inspector who comes to check the property isn't "over" the architect in essence, but the architect properly submits to inspection in his role as resident. Similarly, when the eternal Word entered Mary's womb, He took on human nature and began relating to the Father as "His God"—not because He ceased being divine, but because He genuinely became human and entered into authentic human experience, including the proper human relationship to God.

Supporting Sub-Points:

Sub-point A: The Holy Spirit Spoke Prophetically Through David, But Christ Was the Ultimate Speaker
Shamoun emphasizes that Psalm 22 isn't merely David's words about his own experience with David's son Jesus later applying them to himself. Rather, the Holy Spirit was speaking through David as His instrument, but the ultimate speaker was Christ Himself, prophetically declaring what He would experience and how He would pray during His passion. This is confirmed by multiple New Testament passages that identify Jesus as the primary speaker in messianic psalms (see Hebrews 2:12 citing Psalm 22:22; Hebrews 10:5-7 citing Psalm 40). When Psalm 22:10 says "You are my God from my mother's belly," this is Christ speaking prophetically about His own incarnation centuries before it occurred. This demonstrates both Christ's pre-existence (He could speak of His future incarnation) and the divine inspiration of Scripture (the human author David spoke words whose full meaning he likely didn't completely understand, inspired by the Holy Spirit to prophesy about the Messiah).

Sub-point B: "From My Mother's Belly" Contradicts Abortion's Denial of Personhood
Shamoun makes a pointed contemporary application: Psalm 22:10's phrase "from my mother's belly" directly refutes abortion advocates who deny personhood to the unborn. If Jesus was a person from conception (as evidenced by the Father being His God from that point), then all humans are persons from conception. The teaching explicitly calls abortionists "murderers" because they destroy actual human persons, not merely potential persons or "tissue." This is consistent with Scripture's uniform testimony that human personhood begins at conception: God knew Jeremiah before he was formed in the womb (Jeremiah 1:5); David was sinful from conception (Psalm 51:5), indicating he was a moral agent and therefore a person from that point; John the Baptist was filled with the Holy Spirit from his mother's womb (Luke 1:15). These passages, combined with Psalm 22:10's testimony about when Jesus became human, establish that human life and personhood begin at conception, carrying profound implications for how Christians should view and respond to abortion.

By walking through Psalm 22's complete structure, he reveals that the psalm's movement from lament to praise, from crying out to celebrating deliverance, provides the essential framework for understanding Jesus' quotation. The Father never abandoned the Son; rather, He was actively present, hearing and answering His prayer.

The teaching's brilliance extends beyond mere textual analysis to sophisticated theological synthesis. Shamoun weaves together multiple biblical texts—Psalm 22, Isaiah 53, Isaiah 59, John 1:14, Jeremiah 32:27—showing how they illuminate one another and together support orthodox Christology and Trinitarian theology.
