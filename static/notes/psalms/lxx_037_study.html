Bible Study — Psalm 37 (Orthodox Study Bible)

LXX / OSB: Psalm 37 (Septuagint) | Psalm 38 (Hebrew/Masoretic)

Overview

Psalm 37 (LXX) — Psalm 38 in the Hebrew/MT numbering — is the third of the Church's seven Penitential Psalms (6, 31, 37, 50, 101, 129, 142 LXX), prayed across the Orthodox year as the great scriptural school of repentance. Its Davidic superscription reads εἰς ἀνάμνησιν, περὶ σαββάτου — "for remembrance, concerning the Sabbath" — framing the whole psalm as an act of recollection: the soul deliberately calling its own sin to mind before God rather than fleeing it. The psalm is one sustained lament from inside a body and soul breaking under the weight of guilt: arrows of conviction sunk into the flesh (vv. 2–3), festering wounds (v. 6), a frame bowed to the ground (v. 7), and an inarticulate groaning (στεναγμός) that rises from the heart and is laid bare before God (vv. 9–10). The movement runs from the felt pressure of divine chastisement, through the desolation of abandonment by friends and the chosen silence of one who will not defend himself, to a final confession of iniquity and the bare cry, "Forsake me not." It is a psalm that does not resolve into triumph; it ends still hanging on the edge of need — "make haste to help me" — which is precisely its catechetical genius: it teaches the soul to remain in God even when relief has not yet come.

Section 1 (vv. 2–8): Under the Arrows of Wrath

OSB Notes

The OSB reads the "arrows" (βέλη, v. 3) and the heavy "hand" of God not as vindictive punishment but as chastening — the corrective discipline of a Father who wounds in order to heal (cf. Hebrews 12:6, which the OSB cross-references throughout the penitential Psalter). The festering, stinking wounds of v. 6 are interpreted as the somatic image of sin's corruption: David's outward affliction is the visible sign of an inward decay that sin has worked in the whole person. The OSB notes the refrain "there is no health/peace in my flesh because of my sins" (vv. 4, 8) as the psalm's diagnostic center — sin is not a forensic status only but a sickness coursing through body and soul together, which is why the Orthodox tradition speaks of salvation as healing and the Church as a hospital. The burden "too heavy to bear" (v. 5) is read as the unbearable weight that sin places on a conscience that has not yet laid it down before God.

Theological Themes

Sin as sickness, not merely guilt. The psalm relentlessly somatizes sin: no soundness in the flesh, no peace in the bones, wounds that fester. This is the Orthodox grammar of salvation — sin is a disease of the whole person, and God's "wrath" is experienced as the felt pressure of a corruption that must be healed, not a debt to be paid.

Divine chastisement as mercy. The opening plea ("rebuke me not in Your wrath," v. 2) does not ask God to stop disciplining but to discipline in mercy rather than in anger. The Fathers read this as the soul's recognition that the very pain of conviction is itself a gift — the arrows are aimed by a Physician.

The unbearable weight of unconfessed sin. Verse 5 — iniquities risen "higher than my head," a burden "too heavy" — names the specific suffering of guilt carried alone. The whole psalm is the laying-down of that weight; this section is the weight still being carried.

Patristic & Ascetic Formation

The Father's Reading

Chrysostom, preaching on the penitential Psalms, draws from this opening the teaching that the soul's felt anguish under sin is not something to be anesthetized but to be received — the pain of the wound is the beginning of its healing, because only the soul that feels the festering of its sin will seek the Physician. He insists that David's refusal to minimize his condition ("my wounds stink and fester") is the very posture that opens the door to mercy: the sinner who excuses himself stays sick, while the one who names his rottenness honestly is already turning toward the cure. The arrows, on this reading, are an act of love — God refuses to let the soul grow numb to its own corruption.

Ascetic Movement

This section cultivates compunction (κατάνυξις) — the soul pierced open by awareness of its own condition — and its sustained companion, penthos (πένθος), the holy mourning that follows. The passion it confronts is the self-protective instinct to minimize, excuse, or look away from one's sin. In the three-stage schema this is the rawest work of katharsis: the soul consenting to feel the wound rather than numbing it, allowing the grief over sin to become the energy of repentance rather than the weight of despair.

Orthodox Practice Connection

This section is the interior disposition the Church asks the penitent to bring to Holy Confession: not the cataloguing of offenses from a safe distance, but the willingness to feel the weight before laying it down. Concretely, the catechumen can pray vv. 4–5 slowly as a preparation for Confession — "there is no health in my flesh because of my sin" — letting the words name the actual condition rather than a theoretical one. The Jesus Prayer ("have mercy on me, a sinner") carries the same compunction in miniature, and the great prostrations of Lent enact bodily the bowing-down of v. 7.

LXX Notes

The LXX superscription εἰς ἀνάμνησιν, περὶ σαββάτου ("for remembrance, concerning the Sabbath") has no exact counterpart in the bare MT heading (lehazkir, "for the memorial offering"). The Greek tradition heard in ἀνάμνησις a deliberate act of recollection — the soul summoning its sin to memory before God — a reading that resonates with the same word's liturgical weight in the Eucharistic anamnesis. Otherwise the section's imagery tracks the Hebrew closely; the LXX's relentless "no health in my flesh / no peace in my bones" preserves the somatic theology the Fathers built upon.

Section 2 (vv. 9–15): The Groaning Heard, the Mouth Made Silent

OSB Notes

This is the heart of the psalm and the home of its key word. Verse 9 reads ὠρυόμην ἀπὸ στεναγμοῦ τῆς καρδίας μου — "I roared from the groaning of my heart" — and verse 10, ὁ στεναγμός μου ἀπὸ σοῦ οὐκ ἀπεκρύβη — "my groaning is not hidden from You." The OSB treats this groaning as the wordless prayer of a soul whose anguish has outrun language: a στεναγμός is not a formed petition but the involuntary moan forced out by interior pain, here so intense it rises into a "roar" (ὠρύομαι, the verb used of a lion). The comfort of v. 10 is that this inarticulate groan is already fully known to God — "all my desire is before You." The OSB then notes the desolation that follows: friends and kin stand "afar off" (v. 12) while enemies plot, and David responds not with self-defense but with deliberate silence — "as a deaf man I heard not, as a mute man I opened not my mouth" (vv. 14–15). The Fathers read this silence Christologically, against Isaiah 53:7 ("as a lamb before its shearers is silent"): the righteous sufferer who will not retaliate prefigures Christ silent before His accusers.

Theological Themes

Groaning as wordless prayer. The στεναγμός of vv. 9–10 is prayer beneath the level of speech — the moan of repentance too deep for words. Its theological force is that God receives it: the very anguish the penitent cannot articulate is not hidden from Him. This is the Psalter's anticipation of Romans 8:26, where the Spirit intercedes "with groanings that cannot be uttered" (στεναγμοῖς ἀλαλήτοις).

Abandonment as part of the penitential road. Verses 11–13 — failing strength, lost light of the eyes, friends withdrawn, enemies pressing — name the isolation that often accompanies genuine repentance. The soul facing its sin frequently feels most alone; the psalm refuses to pretend otherwise.

Chosen silence as strength, not weakness. The deaf-mute posture of vv. 14–15 is not passivity but a deliberate refusal to retaliate or self-justify. The penitent who knows his own sin has lost the appetite for vindication; he entrusts his cause to God.

Patristic & Ascetic Formation

The Father's Reading

Athanasius, in his Letter to Marcellinus, assigns this very psalm to the one who is "persecuted by his own kindred" and who, conscious of his sins, will not answer his accusers but commits his cause to God. The Fathers draw from the groaning of vv. 9–10 a profound consolation: when the soul's repentance runs deeper than speech, the groan is the prayer, and God hears precisely it — there is no need to manufacture eloquence before the One from whom nothing is hidden. From the silence of vv. 14–15 Chrysostom draws the ascetic lesson that the truly penitent soul has been freed from the compulsion to defend its reputation: the man who has wept over his own sin no longer has the heart to repay slander with slander, because he sees himself as the chief debtor before God.

Ascetic Movement

This section cultivates two linked dispositions. The groaning of vv. 9–10 is compunction carried to its wordless depth — prayer from the kardia (καρδία) rather than the lips. The silence of vv. 14–15 cultivates makrothumia (μακροθυμία, long-suffering) and prautes (πραΰτης, meekness) — the converting of the soul's irascible energy (θυμός) away from retaliation and toward patient endurance. The passion confronted is the reactive impulse to defend, repay, and vindicate the self. This belongs to the deeper reaches of katharsis: the soul so aware of its own condition before God that it no longer needs to win its case before men.

Orthodox Practice Connection

This section calls the reader toward prayer of the heart in seasons when words fail. When sorrow over sin, or grief, or distress exceeds what can be articulated, the psalm gives permission to simply groan before God in the confidence of v. 10 — "my groaning is not hidden from You" — and to let the wordless ache itself be the prayer. The Jesus Prayer is the disciplined form of this: a single phrase repeated until it descends from lips into the kardia, becoming less a sentence than a continual interior groaning toward Christ. The silence of vv. 14–15 is practiced concretely in the refusal to answer a provocation — offering the unspoken word to God as an ascetic sacrifice.

LXX Notes

The key word of the psalm is the LXX στεναγμός (groaning, from the verb στενάζω), rendering the Hebrew anachah / nahamah (the deep moan of pain). The Greek is theologically decisive because it is the same word St. Paul uses in Romans 8:23 ("we ourselves groan within ourselves") and Romans 8:26 (the Spirit's στεναγμοῖς ἀλαλήτοις, "groanings that cannot be uttered"), and the same word the LXX uses in Exodus 2:24 when God "heard the groaning of the children of Israel." Reading Psalm 37 in Greek therefore knits it into a whole biblical theology of groaning: the moan that God hears and answers (Exodus), the penitent's wordless prayer (here), and the Spirit's own intercession in the believer (Romans). The MT preserves the same sense but cannot reproduce the verbal thread that the LXX makes audible to the patristic ear. In v. 9, the LXX ὠρυόμην ("I roared") intensifies the groan into a lion-like cry, conveying an anguish the calmer English "I am feeble" of Hebrew-based translations softens.

Section 3 (vv. 16–23): Confession and the Cry "Forsake Me Not"

OSB Notes

The psalm turns from lament to hope and confession. Verse 16 anchors everything that follows: ἐπὶ σοί, Κύριε, ἤλπισα — "in You, O Lord, have I hoped; You will hear, O Lord my God." The OSB highlights v. 19 as the decisive penitential act of the whole psalm: ἐγὼ τὴν ἀνομίαν μου ἀναγγελῶ καὶ μεριμνήσω ὑπὲρ τῆς ἁμαρτίας μου — "I will declare my iniquity and be anxious for my sin." This open declaration (ἀναγγελῶ) is the verbal counterpart to the silent groaning of vv. 9–10: what could not be put into words before God is now confessed as sin. The OSB reads vv. 17–18 (the fear that enemies will gloat, the readiness "for scourges") as the soul's continued vulnerability, and the closing triad (vv. 22–23) — "Forsake me not… depart not from me… make haste to help me" — as the bare, unadorned cry of dependence in which the psalm rests its whole weight. The OSB notes that the psalm ends without resolution: David is still waiting, still hoping, the help not yet arrived.

Theological Themes

Hope as the ground beneath confession. Verse 16's "in You I have hoped" precedes and enables the confession of v. 19. The soul can afford to declare its iniquity honestly only because it trusts the One it is confessing to. Confession without hope collapses into despair; hope makes the naming of sin an act of trust rather than self-destruction.

Confession (exomologesis) completes the groaning. What the στεναγμός of vv. 9–10 could only moan, v. 19 now speaks: "I will declare my iniquity." The movement from wordless groan to spoken confession is the interior architecture of the Sacrament of Confession — the soul brings completely out what it had borne within.

Faith that endures unresolved need. The psalm's refusal to end in triumph is its deepest teaching. "Make haste to help me" is the final word: the soul remains in God before deliverance comes, hoping in mercy it has not yet felt. This is the school of patient endurance (hypomonē).

Patristic & Ascetic Formation

The Father's Reading

Chrysostom draws from v. 19 the heart of the penitential life: the difference between the soul that is merely sorry and the soul that confesses. To "declare my iniquity" is to take God's side against oneself — to name one's sin as sin, without minimizing or relocating the blame. He reads "I will be anxious for my sin" not as the anxiety of despair but as the holy carefulness (merimna redirected) of a soul that takes its own salvation seriously. From the unresolved ending the Fathers teach that genuine faith is proven precisely in the gap between prayer and answer: the soul that keeps crying "forsake me not" while help delays is being purified into a trust that no longer depends on consolation.

Ascetic Movement

This section cultivates exomologesis (ἐξομολόγησις, full confession) and hypomonē (ὑπομονή, patient endurance). The groaning of section two becomes articulate self-accusation here — the soul consenting to speak what it had only moaned. The passion confronted is the temptation to despair when relief is delayed (athymia, ἀθυμία). The remedy the psalm models is to keep praying into the silence: the unresolved "make haste to help me" is itself the act of faith that purifies. This is katharsis maturing toward trust — the soul learning to remain in God on the strength of hope alone.

Orthodox Practice Connection

This section calls the reader most directly toward the Sacrament of Holy Confession. Verse 19 — "I will declare my iniquity" — is the scriptural shape of the sacrament: the silent burden of sin, borne and groaned over interiorly, is at last brought into the light and spoken aloud before God in the presence of the priest. The catechumen preparing for reception can pray vv. 16 and 19 together as the two halves of a right confession: first the hope ("in You, O Lord, have I hoped"), then the naming ("I will declare my iniquity"). And when prayers seem to go unanswered, the psalm's closing cry — "Forsake me not, O Lord… make haste to help me" — becomes a model for remaining in God through the delay, neither abandoning prayer nor demanding that God act on one's own schedule.

LXX Notes

Verse 19's ἀναγγελῶ ("I will declare/announce") is stronger than a private admission — it carries the sense of a public, outward proclamation, which the Fathers connect to the externalizing character of exomologesis (the sin brought out into the light, not merely acknowledged within). The pairing with μεριμνήσω ("I will be anxious/take thought") is notable: the LXX uses the same root (merimna) that elsewhere names the passion of anxious worry, but here it is consecrated — anxiety redirected from the world's cares onto the one thing that rightly merits such concern, the state of one's own soul. The closing verbs of vv. 22–23 (μὴ ἐγκαταλίπῃς "forsake not," μὴ ἀποστῇς "depart not," πρόσχες "attend/make haste") build a threefold crescendo of dependence that the patristic tradition heard as the very pattern of the soul's final reliance on grace.

Synthesis

Psalm 37 (LXX) calls you to learn the whole architecture of repentance — not as a single act of saying sorry, but as a movement that begins in the body's felt anguish under sin, passes through a groaning too deep for words, and arrives at last at open confession spoken into the light. This psalm refuses to let you anesthetize the wound: it insists that the festering you feel (vv. 4–6) is the Physician's arrow, that the weight too heavy to carry (v. 5) is meant to be laid down and not merely endured. Its hinge is the groaning (στεναγμός) of vv. 9–10 — the moan of a heart whose repentance has outrun its vocabulary — and its great consolation is that this very groan is not hidden from God. The Fathers heard in that word the same groaning the Spirit prays within us (Romans 8:26) and the same groaning God heard in Egypt and answered (Exodus 2:24); to pray this psalm is to discover that your wordless ache is already a prayer fully received. From there the psalm teaches the silence that will not retaliate (vv. 14–15) — the meekness of a soul too aware of its own debt to repay slander — and finally the confession that names sin as sin (v. 19) on the strength of a hope declared first (v. 16). It ends, deliberately, unresolved: "make haste to help me." This is the psalm's last and hardest gift — it forms in you a faith that remains in God before the rescue arrives, a trust that keeps crying into the silence and is purified in the very crying. The groaning that God hears is not the groan of one who has been delivered, but of one who hopes, still waiting, that He will not forsake.

Discussion Questions

  1. The psalm describes sin in physical, almost medical terms — festering wounds, no health in the flesh, a frame bowed to the ground (vv. 4–7). How does thinking of sin as a sickness to be healed rather than only a debt to be paid change the way you approach repentance and Confession?

  2. Verse 2 asks God not to stop rebuking but to rebuke "not in wrath" — receiving the very pain of conviction as a Father's discipline. Where in your life have you experienced a painful chastening that, in hindsight, was an act of mercy?

  3. The "groaning of the heart" (vv. 9–10) is wordless prayer — anguish too deep for speech, yet "not hidden" from God. Have you experienced prayer that could not be put into words? What does it mean to you that God receives the groan itself, before any sentence is formed?

  4. In vv. 14–15 David chooses to be "as a mute man," refusing to defend himself against his accusers. When is silence in the face of provocation an act of strength rather than weakness — and when is it merely avoidance? How do you tell the difference?

  5. Verse 19 — "I will declare my iniquity" — turns the silent groaning into open confession. Why does the psalm move from wordless groaning (vv. 9–10) to spoken declaration (v. 19)? What does this suggest about why the Church asks us to confess sin aloud rather than only inwardly?

  6. The psalm ends without resolution: "Forsake me not… make haste to help me." Why might it be spiritually important that this prayer ends still waiting, rather than in triumph? Where are you currently being asked to keep hoping in God before the help has arrived?
