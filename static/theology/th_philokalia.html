The Philokalia

Love of the beautiful — the great anthology of hesychast prayer

Orthodox Patristics Tier II · Study Note

What Is the Philokalia

The Philokalia (Φιλοκαλία — "love of the beautiful" or "love of the good") is a collection of texts on prayer, watchfulness, and the interior life, written by Eastern Christian monks and Fathers from the 4th to the 15th century. Compiled in the 18th century, it is the foundational treasury of hesychast spirituality and the primary guide to νοερὰ προσευχή — prayer of the intellect.

The title points to the goal of the collection: the beautiful (τὸ καλόν) in the Platonic-Christian sense is God Himself, the source of all beauty. To love the Philokalia is to love the path toward God.

History & Transmission

The collection was compiled by St. Nikodimos of the Holy Mountain and St. Makarios of Corinth and first published in Venice in 1782. The two compilers drew on manuscripts preserved on Mount Athos that had largely been inaccessible to the wider Church.

Within a decade, St. Paisios Velichkovsky produced a Slavonic translation, the Dobrotolubiye (Добротолюбіє), which spread hesychast teaching throughout Russia and the Slavic world. A fuller Russian edition followed in the 19th century under Bishop Theophan the Recluse. The standard English translation — five volumes by Palmer, Sherrard, and Ware — was completed between 1979 and 1995.

"The Philokalia is a school of prayer. It does not replace the Church or the sacraments — it teaches you how to enter them more deeply." — Kallistos Ware, Introduction to the Philokalia, Vol. I

Contents & Key Authors

The collection spans roughly 36 authors across eleven centuries. The texts are not systematic theology but practical guides — written by and for monks, yet applicable to any serious Christian life. Key figures include:

  • Evagrios Pontikos (4th c.) — foundational teaching on the passions (πάθη) and the eight logismoi; theory of pure prayer.
  • Diadochos of Photike (5th c.) — on spiritual knowledge (γνῶσις) and the use of the Jesus Prayer as a single-pointed focus for the nous.
  • John Climacus (6th–7th c.) — The Ladder of Divine Ascent; systematic treatment of virtue, the passions, and hesychia.
  • Maximos the Confessor (7th c.) — on love (ἀγάπη), theosis, and the cosmic dimensions of ascetic life.
  • Symeon the New Theologian (10th–11th c.) — on conscious experience of divine light and the necessity of a spiritual father.
  • Gregory Palamas (14th c.) — the theological foundation for all hesychasm: the real distinction between divine essence and energies, and the deifying reality of the uncreated light.

Core Themes

The Philokalia is unified by a single practical concern: the purification of the νοῦς (intellect/heart) so that it can perceive God. The path is consistently described in three movements.

Greek

κάθαρσις · φωτισμός · θέωσις

English

Purification · Illumination · Deification

Central to this ascent is νῆψις (nepsis — watchfulness or sobriety): the attentive guarding of the nous against intrusive thoughts (λογισμοί). The practitioner learns to observe thoughts without identifying with them, and to return the mind to the heart through the Jesus Prayer.

1 Thessalonians 5:17
"Pray without ceasing."

This apostolic injunction is taken literally in the Philokalia tradition. The Jesus Prayer — "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner" — is the primary vehicle for unceasing prayer, and its practice is detailed across dozens of texts in the collection.

Key Vocabulary

  • Νοῦς (nous) — the intellect or spiritual heart; the faculty capable of direct perception of God, distinct from discursive reason (διάνοια).
  • Ἡσυχία (hesychia) — stillness, inner quiet; the state sought through watchfulness and prayer.
  • Νῆψις (nepsis) — watchfulness, sobriety; the alert guarding of the nous against logismoi.
  • Λογισμοί (logismoi) — intrusive thoughts or temptations; the eight foundational passions classified by Evagrios.
  • Ἀπάθεια (apatheia) — freedom from the passions; not emotional numbness but ordered affectivity, the nous undistorted by passion.
  • Θεωρία (theoria) — contemplation; in the Philokalia, the vision of divine light, not mere intellectual speculation.

For the Catechumen

The Philokalia is often misread as a monastic-only text. Its compilers intended it otherwise: Nikodimos wrote explicitly that its teachings are for "every Christian who desires to be saved." The degree of practice differs between monk and layperson; the direction does not.

A catechumen approaching the Philokalia for the first time should begin with Volume I — particularly the texts of Diadochos of Photike and the short works on the Jesus Prayer — rather than beginning with Evagrios, whose vocabulary is technical and easily misread without patristic context. Read alongside the Jesus Prayer practice itself, not in abstraction from it.

"Do not seek to experience the things spoken of in this book before you have acquired a spiritual father." — Nikodimos of the Holy Mountain, Preface to the Philokalia
