The Religion of the Apostles — Chapter 1

Divine Father and Son: identifying Christ as the Second Hypostasis of Yahweh

Orthodox Christology Patristics Church History Tier III · Analysis
"Let us approach the Scriptures with great attention — not opening them carelessly, but receiving what is there rather than what we have already decided to find." — St. John Chrysostom, Homilies on John, Hom. 1
Before You Read
This chapter asks you to set aside a historical framework you have likely absorbed without knowing it — the idea that Trinitarian doctrine was invented gradually by later Christians. That is not a small ask. Read the section on Second Temple Judaism's "second power in heaven" without rushing to reconcile it with what you already believe. De Young is recovering something that was always in the text. The goal is not to win an argument but to read Scripture the way the Apostles did.

Chapter Overview

De Young opens with a direct assault on the most common assumption in modern biblical scholarship: that Old Testament Israel was unitarily monotheist and that Trinitarian doctrine was a gradual Christian invention imposed on earlier, simpler belief. His central argument is that this narrative is simply false. Israel's faith in Yahweh was never unitarian — the Hebrew Scriptures themselves present Yahweh as existing in multiple ὑποστάσεις (Persons), and Second Temple Judaism widely debated the identity and nature of this "second power in heaven."

If De Young is right, then Nicene Trinitarianism and high Christology are not Greek philosophical overlays on Hebrew monotheism but clarifications and fulfillment of what Israel had always believed. The entire chapter is an exercise in reading the Old Testament through the lens the Apostles themselves would have used — and finding Christ already there.

1. The Dominant Narrative Is Wrong

Core Argument

The standard scholarly story — that OT Israel was unitarian, early Christians had "low Christology," and Trinitarian doctrine evolved over centuries — is historically incorrect.

Historical Context

This narrative gained dominance through 19th–20th century historical-critical scholarship (Harnack, Bultmann, and more recently Bart Ehrman's How Jesus Became God). It treats theological development as analogous to biological evolution: primitive → sophisticated. The critical error is reading Rabbinic Judaism (post-2nd century) as the norm for all pre-Christian Jewish thought.

Could Christianity be considered the continuation of Second Temple Judaism — and Rabbinic Judaism the branch off?

Biblical Foundation

De Young points to passages throughout the Pentateuch and Prophets where Yahweh appears in multiple interacting Persons — most strikingly, texts where Yahweh addresses Himself or where two figures are simultaneously identified as Yahweh (Gen. 18; Judg. 6; Ex. 23:20–23).

Patristic Witness

St. Justin Martyr (Dialogue with Trypho, 61) explicitly argues from the Hebrew Scriptures that Yahweh begat a rational Power before all creatures, called variously Glory, Son, Wisdom, Angel, God, Lord, and Logos — an argument he presents as inherited, not invented.

Sub-Points

  • Arianism and Adoptionism were not Christian inventions but continuations of already-existing Second Temple strands of thought.
  • The 4th-century councils did not introduce new ideas; they defended apostolic teaching against these earlier alternatives.
  • Unitarian monotheism in Judaism began as a reaction to Christianity in the 2nd century, not as original OT religion.
Catechumenate Note
For someone preparing for Baptism, this chapter establishes that becoming Orthodox does not mean converting away from the Old Testament but rather receiving it in its fullness. The catechumen who wonders "why does Christianity look so different from Judaism?" receives here a historical answer: Rabbinic Judaism is the innovation. The apostolic faith is the original.

2. The Angel of the Lord as Second Hypostasis

Core Argument

The "Malakh Yahweh" (Angel of the Lord) throughout the Torah is identified as Yahweh Himself while simultaneously being distinguished from the Father — evidence of two Persons within the one Godhead.

Biblical Foundation

Exodus 3:2, 4
The "Angel of the Lord" appears in the burning bush, but "God" speaks — the same figure.
Judges 6:11–23
The Angel of the Lord and Yahweh speak interchangeably to Gideon; after the Angel departs, Yahweh remains.
Exodus 23:20–23
God places His Name in this Angel; Israel must obey Him as they obey God.
Judges 2:1
The Angel claims to have led Israel out of Egypt — a deed attributed to Yahweh in Mosaic texts.

Patristic Witness

St. Paul identifies this Angel as Christ: "the rock that followed them was Christ" (1 Cor. 10:3–4). Jude 1:5 (in earliest manuscripts) says Jesus "saved a people out of Egypt." The Fathers read all OT theophanies as appearances of the pre-incarnate Son.

Sub-Points

  • The Angel is not a created being — Hebrew malakh means "messenger" and describes function, not nature.
  • When the Angel departs (Judg. 2:1–5), "the Word of the Lord was rare" — suggesting the Angel and the Word are the same figure.
  • God placing His Name "in" the Angel (Ex. 23:21) becomes St. John's template: the Father is "in" Christ (John 14:11, 20; 17:23).
Catechumenate Note
Many catechumens come from Protestant backgrounds where the OT and NT feel like different books about different Gods. This section shows the continuity: the same Person who appeared to Abraham, Moses, and Gideon is the one who walked in Galilee. Baptism is initiation into the fullness of this God's covenant.

3. The Word of the Lord as Personal Hypostasis

Core Argument

The phrase "Debar Yahweh" (Word of the Lord) in the Hebrew Scriptures refers not to Scripture or inspiration but to a divine Person who appeared bodily to the prophets.

Historical Context

Modern Christian usage conflates "Word of God" with "Scripture" — a usage the Bible never employs for itself. In Second Temple Aramaic translations (Targums), this confusion was avoided by using Memra ("Word") specifically to describe the divine figure seen by the patriarchs. The Targum translators were solving a theological problem: how could the unknowable God be seen? Their answer: humans saw the Memra, the second hypostasis.

Biblical Foundation

Genesis 15:1
The "Word of Yahweh appeared to Abram in a vision" and identified Himself as "the Lord Yahweh" (v. 2).
1 Samuel 3:1–21
The Word of the Lord was "rare" and not "revealed" to Samuel — a person can only be revealed, not a text; Yahweh stood by Samuel's bed and Samuel was afraid to tell Eli his "vision."
Jeremiah 1:4–9
The Word of the Lord came, spoke, and then stretched out His hand and touched Jeremiah's mouth.
Psalm 33/32:6
"By the Word of Yahweh the heavens were made, and by the Breath (Ruach/Pnevma) of His mouth all their hosts."

Patristic Witness

St. John's prologue (In the beginning was the Word) is not philosophical Platonism but the exegesis of this exact OT tradition, identifying Jesus Christ as the Memra/Debar Yahweh who appeared to Abraham, Moses, and the prophets, now become flesh (John 1:14).

Sub-Points

  • Philo of Alexandria also identified the Memra/Logos as the Angel of the Lord — evidence that this was mainstream Second Temple reading, not Christian innovation (Somn. 1.228–39).
  • Philo integrated the Logos into Platonic philosophy; St. John identified the Logos as the person he had personally known as Jesus of Nazareth — personal witness vs. philosophical speculation.
  • John 1:18 — "No one has ever seen God, but the unique God at the side of the Father, He has made Him known" — identifies who was seen in the OT: the Son.
Catechumenate Note
For the catechumen, this section transforms Bible reading. Every OT passage where "the Word of the Lord came to" a prophet is an encounter with the pre-incarnate Christ. The OT is not preparation for Someone who hasn't arrived yet — it is the testimony of those who already knew Him, however dimly.

4. The Wisdom of God in Proverbs 8

Core Argument

Proverbs 8's personified Wisdom, who was "beside [God] as a master craftsman" before and during Creation, is the same divine hypostasis as the Word of the Lord — the pre-eternal Son — and is the direct textual source for the Creed's phrase "begotten before all worlds."

Historical Context

The Arian controversy of the 4th century centered on this passage. Arians argued that Wisdom was created before the rest of creation. The Orthodox replied that the Hebrew verbs do not imply creation from nothing but a begetting-relationship: qanah (possessed/acquired — Eve's verb for bearing a child, Gen. 4:1) and chul (to writhe/be born in labor, vv. 24–25) reveal a begetting, not a creating.

Biblical Foundation

  • Prov. 8:22–30 — Wisdom was "with Him" before the earth, a "master craftsman" alongside Yahweh in Creation.
  • John 1:1–3 — The Word was "with God" and all things were made through Him — direct parallel.
  • Col. 1:15–17 — Christ is "Firstborn over all creation" and "in Him all things hold together."
  • 1 Cor. 1:24 — "Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God."
  • Gen. 1:2 — The Spirit (Ruach) hovers over the waters — Ps. 33/32:6 unites Father (mouth), Word, and Breath in the act of Creation.

Patristic Witness

The Fathers (Athanasius, Basil, Gregory the Theologian) consistently interpreted Prov. 8 as speaking of the eternal Son, begotten before all worlds. When they say "begotten, not made" in the Creed, they are doing the same exegesis De Young reconstructs. The great cathedral of Constantinople — Hagia Sophia — is dedicated to Christ, the Wisdom of God incarnate.

Sub-Points

  • Monogenes (John 1:18; 3:16) is better translated "unique" than "only-begotten," but Prov. 8's chul preserves the begetting imagery the Creed intends.
  • The Fathers' analogy of Father → mouth, Son → word, Spirit → breath is exegesis of Ps. 33:6, not Greek philosophical invention.
  • Hagia Sophia is dedicated to Christ, the Wisdom of God — an iconographic embodiment of this exegetical tradition.
Catechumenate Note
The pre-baptismal rites include the Creed. When the catechumen confesses "begotten before all ages," they are entering into this ancient tradition — affirming not a Greek philosophical innovation but the meaning embedded in Proverbs.

5. The Son of Man as Divine-Human Figure

Core Argument

Christ's most frequent self-title, "Son of Man," refers not to His humanity alone but to the specific divine-human figure from Daniel 7 — already recognized in Second Temple literature as the second hypostasis of Yahweh and the Messiah. Claiming this title was tantamount to claiming to be God.

Historical Context

Daniel 7's vision of one "like a Son of Man" riding the clouds and receiving universal authority was extensively developed in Second Temple texts (1 Enoch, 4 Ezra). Cloud-riding was exclusively divine imagery in the ancient Near East. At His trial (Mark 14:61–63), Christ's self-identification as this figure prompted Caiaphas to tear his garments for blasphemy — not because He claimed to be the Davidic Messiah but because He claimed divinity.

Biblical Foundation

Daniel 7:13–14
"One like a Son of Man" comes before the Ancient of Days on the clouds and receives all ἐξουσία (authority).
Matthew 28:18
"All authority (ἐξουσία) in heaven and on earth has been given to me" — the direct fulfillment of Daniel's vision.
Mark 14:61–63
Caiaphas tears his garments at Christ's self-identification as Son of Man coming on clouds.

Sub-Points

  • "Son of Adam" (ben adam) in the OT recalls human mortality and fragility — Daniel's figure is both mortal man and divine cloud-rider: fully human and fully divine.
  • First Advent (Incarnation, Cross, Resurrection, Ascension) = receiving the ἐξουσία of the nations; Second Advent = executing judgment with that authority.
  • The Church's mission in the present age is between these two Advents: the Kingdom is established, but the nations continue "for a time" (Dan. 7:12).
Catechumenate Note
Baptism is entry into the Kingdom of the Son of Man. The catechumen does not merely join a religious institution but is incorporated into the eternal dominion already received by Christ at the Ascension — which is why the Creed ends with the expectation of His return.

6. The Body of God and Bodily Appearances

Core Argument

Yahweh's consistent bodily appearances throughout the OT (walking, eating, wrestling, writing with His finger) were understood by early Christianity as appearances of the pre-incarnate Son — and the Rabbinic denial that "God has a body" was a late anti-Christian innovation, not original Jewish teaching.

Historical Context

The synagogue hymn "God does not have a body" was added in the 4th–5th century precisely to reject the Christian interpretation. Justin Martyr, Basil the Great, and Origen all testify that in their respective eras, Jewish teaching still acknowledged God had a form or body. Maimonides' codification of bodilessness as the 13th Principle of Faith came still later — in the 13th century.

Biblical Foundation

  • Gen. 18 — Yahweh and two angels eat with Abraham at Mamre; Yahweh deliberates with Himself (18:17–19).
  • Gen. 32:24–30 — The "man" who wrestles Jacob gives him the name Israel; Jacob names the place "the face of God."
  • Ex. 33:11, 20–23 — God speaks with Moses "face to face as a friend," yet Moses cannot see His face and live — the tension resolved by two hypostases: one seen, one unseeable.
  • Is. 6:1; Ez. 1:26–27 — Prophets describe God enthroned with physical characteristics.
  • Ex. 31:18 — God writes the Commandments with His own finger.

Patristic Witness

The Orthodox iconographic tradition of the Hospitality of Abraham (Andrei Rublev's Trinity icon) depicts the three visitors at Mamre as an icon of the Holy Trinity — the beginning of the Trinitarian revelation culminated at Pentecost.

Sub-Points

  • The tension in Ex. 33 ("face to face" AND "you cannot see my face and live") is the same text that generated the Second Temple doctrine of two hypostases in Yahweh.
  • Christ is the hypostasis who was seen; the Father is the unseen hypostasis — hence John 1:18 and John 14:9.
  • The Fathers insist it was the Son, not the Father, who was seen in the OT — guarding against Patripassianism while affirming the Son's full divinity.
Catechumenate Note
The pre-baptismal instruction on icons often troubles catechumens from iconoclastic Protestant backgrounds. This section provides the deepest answer: we depict Christ because the entire OT already depicted Him, in bodily appearances to the Fathers and Prophets.

7. Jesus Christ Is Yahweh — Anti-Marcionite Foundation

Core Argument

Before turning to NT evidence, De Young addresses a perennial threat from within: Marcionism — ancient and modern. The heresy that the wrathful OT God differs from the loving NT Father is refuted not only by showing OT love and mercy, but equally by showing NT judgment and wrath. The argument works in both directions. Hebrews 13:8 anchors it: Christ is the same yesterday, today, and forever.

Historical Context

Marcion of Sinope (ca. AD 85–160) taught that Yahweh and the Father of Christ were different Gods, producing a truncated canon. De Young's concern, however, is soft Marcionism — the contemporary tendency to domesticate Christ by over-emphasizing His love while ignoring His wrath, or treating the OT as spiritually inferior. This produces a partial Christ who cannot call anyone to genuine repentance.

Biblical Foundation

  • Heb. 13:8 — "Jesus Christ is the same yesterday, today, and forever."
  • Rev. 6:16 — "the wrath of the Lamb."
  • Matt. 23 — Christ's fierce denunciation of the scribes and Pharisees.
  • John 15:1–6 — Christ cuts off and burns fruitless branches.
  • Lev. 19:18; Deut. 6:5 — The commands to love God and neighbor originate in the Torah.

Key Formulation

"The Christ who walked in the Garden and cursed the serpent, who destroyed Sodom and Gomorrah, who led the people out of Egypt and commanded Joshua's armies is the same Christ whom we encounter in the four Gospels." — Fr. Stephen De Young, The Religion of the Apostles, Ch. 1

The icon of the Pantocrator — gentle eyes, severe face — is the visual theology of this balance: mercy and severity held together in one divine Person.

Catechumenate Note
The Creed the catechumen will confess includes "He shall come again with glory to judge the living and the dead." Orthodox formation includes a reckoning with the whole Christ — the Christ of mercy who is also the Christ of fire and coming wrath.

8. The Divine Christ in the Gospels

Core Argument

The four Gospels present a consistent, coherent picture of Christ as the second hypostasis of the God of Israel incarnate — not four Christologies at different stages of development. St. John does so through Christ's explicit verbal self-identifications; St. Mark does so through His deeds — particularly walking on water, which directly alludes to Yahweh walking on the waves in Job 9 LXX.

Historical Context

The dominant scholarly paradigm reads the Synoptics as "low Christology" and John as "high Christology," arranging them as stages of development. The discovery of the Rylands Papyrus (P52) — a fragment of John's Gospel datable to the first quarter of the second century — foreclosed very late datings. De Young's argument: the methodological circularity of reading developmental presuppositions into the texts produces the appearance of development where there is only diversity of literary approach.

Biblical Foundation

John 8:56–59
"Before Abraham was, I am" — the crowd picks up stones immediately for blasphemy.
Mark 6:45–52
Christ walks on water; He "went to pass by them" — alludes to Job 9:8, 11 LXX (Yahweh walking on the waves; παρελθεῖν).
John's Approach

Christ's words identify Him as Yahweh. The absolute ἐγώ εἰμι of John 8:58 echoes Ex. 3:14 LXX — so clear the audience reaches for stones without deliberation.

Mark's Approach

Christ's deeds identify Him as Yahweh. περιπατῶν ἐπὶ θαλάσσης and παρελθεῖν in Mark 6:48 are direct verbal allusions to Job 9:8, 11 LXX, where these actions belong to Yahweh alone.

Catechumenate Note
Catechumens from Evangelical backgrounds are often most at home in the Gospels but have been formed to read Christ primarily as moral teacher. This section opens a deeper lens: the Jesus of Mark's Gospel is the same divine Person who appeared in the burning bush, walked on the primeval sea in Job's meditation, and will return on the clouds in Daniel's vision. Reading the Gospels with this awareness transforms them from biography into sustained theophany.

9. Saint Paul's Divine Christ

Core Argument

St. Paul's Epistles are the earliest written NT documents — earlier than any Gospel — and they already contain fully-formed, sophisticated Christology. The Carmen Christi (Phil. 2:5–11) presents Christ as a pre-existent divine Person who self-emptied in the Incarnation. First Corinthians 8:6 inserts Christ into the Shema itself, identifying Him as the second hypostasis of the one God of Israel.

Historical Context

The developmental thesis predicts: Paul (earliest, most primitive) → Synoptics → John (latest, most developed). But Philippians, Corinthians, and Galatians predate all four Gospels and already contain what the developmental view would label "high Christology." Phil. 2:5–11 may represent a pre-Pauline hymn, placing this Christology within years of the Resurrection.

Biblical Foundation

Philippians 2:5–11 — Carmen Christi
Christ "existing in divine form" (μορφῇ θεοῦ) empties Himself to take the form of a servant; at His exaltation every knee bows and every tongue confesses Κύριος — the divine Name from Is. 45:23.
1 Corinthians 8:6 — The Modified Shema
"One God, the Father, from whom are all things… and one Lord, Jesus Christ, through whom are all things." Christ is inserted at precisely the point where the divine Name (Κύριος for YHWH) belongs in the Shema.
1 Corinthians 10:3–4
"The rock was Christ" — Israel's wilderness companion identified as Christ.

Key Observation

Μορφὴ θεοῦ (divine form) is contrasted with μορφὴ δούλου (form of a servant). If "form of a servant" means truly human, the grammatical and theological parallel requires "divine form" to mean truly divine.

Patristic Witness

St. John Chrysostom (Homilies on Philippians, Hom. 7) reads μορφὴ θεοῦ as full divinity. St. Basil the Great (On the Holy Spirit, §16) notes that 2 Cor. 13:14's Trinitarian benediction presupposes the full divinity of all three Persons by giving each equal share in the apostolic blessing.

Catechumenate Note
The catechumen who has been told that "the Trinity was invented at Nicaea" now has a decisive counter-testimony: the Trinitarian pattern is present in Paul's Epistles, written two to three decades after the Resurrection and three centuries before Nicaea. The Council did not invent; it defended what the Apostles already taught.

10. The Divine Christ in the General Epistles

Core Argument

The General Epistles — James, 1–2 Peter, 1–3 John, Jude — are eclectic in style and audience, but unanimous in their Christology: Jesus Christ is the second hypostasis of Yahweh incarnate. James calls Him "the Lord of Glory" (2:1, citing Ps. 24). Second Peter calls Him "our God and Savior" (1:1). Jude identifies Him as the Angel of the Lord who delivered Israel from Egypt (v. 5).

Biblical Foundation

  • James 2:1 — "the Lord of Glory" — citing Ps. 24/23 (Yahweh as "King of Glory").
  • 2 Pet. 1:1 — "the righteousness of our God and Savior Jesus Christ."
  • Jude 1:5 — "Jesus saved a people out of Egypt and afterward destroyed those who did not believe" (earliest manuscripts).
  • 1 John 5:20 — "This is the true God and eternal life" — referring to the Son.

Sub-Points

  • The diversity of these letters — different audiences, dates, styles — makes their Christological unity all the more significant.
  • Jude 1:5: the reading "Jesus" is likely original — the lectio difficilior; a scribe changes "Jesus" to "God" to avoid confusion, not the reverse.
  • 1 Peter and Jude draw on the Enochic tradition, which identified the Son of Man of Daniel 7 as a divine figure — confirming these letters are reading Christ into a pre-existing Jewish theological framework.
Catechumenate Note
The catechumen will encounter these epistles at services and may find them difficult to place. Beneath the surface of each is the same confession: Jesus Christ is the God of Israel, active in history, returning as Judge, and the only source of eternal life.

11. Christ in the Apocalypse

Core Argument

The Book of Revelation is the Ἀποκάλυψις — the Revelation — of Jesus Christ, not primarily of future events. Its subject is who Christ is. The book distributes OT prophetic descriptions of Yahweh between the Father (described only by the throne itself) and Christ (the Lamb and Son of Man, described with language drawn from Daniel 10 and Ezekiel 1). Both share identical titles (Alpha and Omega; Lord God Almighty), receive identical worship from the heavenly council, and share the one throne.

Biblical Foundation

Revelation 1:8
"I am the Alpha and the Omega, says the Lord God, who is and who was and who is coming, the Almighty" (the Father speaking).
Revelation 5:9–13
The Lamb receives identical worship from the heavenly council — "Worthy is the Lamb" — parallel to "Worthy are you, our Lord and God" (4:11) directed to the Father.
Revelation 22:13–16
Christ identifies Himself as "the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end" — the same title used by Yahweh in Rev. 1:8 and Is. 44:6.

Sub-Points

  • The OT descriptions of enthroned Yahweh (Ezekiel 1, Daniel 7) are distributed between Father (the throne; no physical description) and Christ (described with those same prophetic images) — communicating that the visible God of the OT was always the Son.
  • The heavenly council sings "Worthy is the Lamb" with vocabulary parallel to "Worthy are you, our Lord and God." Worship is uniquely the prerogative of God; St. John is twice corrected when he worships the angel (19:10; 22:8–9), but not when he falls before Christ (1:17).
  • "The wrath of the Lamb" (6:16) and "the wrath of God" (14:10) function as synonyms throughout Revelation. The throne is simultaneously "of God and of the Lamb" (22:1).

The Orthodox Divine Liturgy is a participation in the heavenly liturgy St. John describes. The Pantocrator icon — Christ reigning in the dome — is the visual translation of Revelation 1:12–16: the Lamb who was slain and is enthroned, now looking down upon His assembled people. Every Liturgy is a gathering before the throne of God and of the Lamb.

Catechumenate Note
The catechumen is preparing to enter the community that gathers before the Lamb. The Orthodox understanding of the Church is precisely this: the earthly image of the heavenly assembly around the throne. Baptism and Chrismation are not enrollment in an institution but initiation into this eternal worship — "Worthy is the Lamb that was slain" is now their song as well.

Bible Verse Deep Dives

Exodus 3:2, 4 — The Burning Bush

The grammar distinguishes the Angel of the Lord (v. 2) from the speaker who identifies Himself as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (v. 4–6). Two Persons, one God. The LXX renders Malakh Yahweh as Ἄγγελος Κυρίου — carrying directly into NT usage of the same second-hypostasis figure. Cross-references: Ex. 23:20–23; Judg. 2:1; 1 Cor. 10:4.

Genesis 15:1 — The Word Appears to Abram

The "Word of Yahweh appeared to Abram in a vision" — a sensory/visual event, not merely an intellectual impression. The Word identifies Himself as Yahweh who brought Abram from Ur. The Targum (Memra d'Yahweh) consistently uses this term wherever the Hebrew text involves direct divine encounter — naming the Second Person who was seen. Cross-references: Gen. 15:4–7; John 1:1–3; John 8:56.

Proverbs 8:22–30 — Wisdom as Master Craftsman

The verbs qanah (v. 22) and chul (vv. 24–25) describe a begetting relationship, not creation ex nihilo. The LXX renders qanah as ἔκτισεν (created/founded) — which Arius weaponized. The Orthodox response: chul implies begetting, and ἔκτισεν in Greek can mean "brought forth" as well as "created." Cross-references: John 1:1–3; Col. 1:15–17; 1 Cor. 1:24.

Daniel 7:13–14 — One Like a Son of Man

Cloud-riding imagery is exclusively divine in the ancient Near East; the human form is simultaneously emphasized — the clearest OT anticipation of the Incarnation. The Theodotion LXX renders bar enash as υἱὸς ἀνθρώπου — exactly the formula Christ uses. Cross-references: Matt. 28:18; Mark 14:61–63; Rev. 1:13; Acts 1:9–11.

Exodus 33:11, 20–23 — Face to Face / Cannot See My Face

In the same chapter, God speaks with Moses "face to face" AND declares no one can see His face and live. Orthodox resolution: Moses spoke face to face with the Son; the Father's face is unseeable. The LXX renders panim el panim as πρόσωπον κατὰ πρόσωπον — the same phrase used in 1 Cor. 13:12 for our eschatological vision of God. Cross-references: John 1:18; John 14:9; Is. 6:1; Ez. 1:26.

John 8:56–59 — "Before Abraham Was, I Am"

Christ's πρὶν Ἀβραὰμ γενέσθαι, ἐγώ εἰμι is a deliberate claim to the divine Name of Ex. 3:14 LXX (Ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν). The audience's immediate reaction — taking up stones without a further word — confirms they understood precisely what He claimed. Any first-century Jewish hearer formed on the LXX would have caught it immediately. Cross-references: Ex. 3:14 LXX; John 18:5–6; John 17:5; Is. 43:10–13.

Mark 6:48 — Walking on Water / "Going to Pass By"

The Greek verb παρελθεῖν (to pass by) and περιπατῶν ἐπὶ θαλάσσης (walking on the sea) are direct verbal allusions to Job 9:8, 11 LXX, where both actions belong exclusively to Yahweh. St. Mark places the disciples in the role of Job and Christ in the role of Yahweh. The LXX of Job 9:8 reads περιπατῶν ὡς ἐπ' ἐδάφους ἐπὶ θαλάσσης — precisely the language Mark echoes. Cross-references: Job 9:8, 11; Ps. 77/76:19; 1 Kg. 19:11.

Philippians 2:5–11 — The Carmen Christi

The LXX of Is. 45:23 reads ἐμοὶ κάμψει πᾶν γόνυ… καὶ ἐξομολογήσεται πᾶσα γλῶσσα τῷ θεῷ ("every knee shall bow to me… every tongue shall confess to God"). St. Paul applies this directly to Christ in Phil. 2:10–11, replacing τῷ θεῷ with the name Ἰησοῦς and Κύριος — inserting Christ into the most solemn Yahweh-sovereignty confession in the Hebrew Scriptures. Cross-references: Is. 45:23; John 1:1–3; Col. 1:15–17.

1 Corinthians 8:6 — The Modified Shema

The Shema of Deut. 6:4 in Greek: Κύριος ὁ θεὸς ἡμῶν, Κύριος εἷς ἐστιν. Paul distributes the one Yahweh between two hypostases — the Father (θεός) and Christ (Κύριος) — inserting Christ at precisely the point where the divine Name belongs. Κύριος carried the full weight of the Tetragrammaton for any Greek-speaking Jew. Cross-references: Deut. 6:4 LXX; Phil. 2:10–11; 1 Cor. 12:4–6.

Revelation 22:13–16 — Alpha and Omega

Christ identifies Himself as "the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end" — the same title used by Yahweh in Rev. 1:8 and Is. 44:6. The LXX of Is. 44:6: ἐγὼ πρῶτος καὶ ἐγὼ μετὰ ταῦτα — a divine self-declaration of exclusive sovereignty that Revelation applies to both Father and Son. One title, one divine nature, two Persons — the Nicene doctrine in a single image. Cross-references: Rev. 1:8; Rev. 1:17–18; Is. 44:6; Is. 41:4; Rev. 5:13.

Orthodox Lens

Liturgical Connection

  • "Wisdom! Let us be attentive" (Σοφία! Πρόσχωμεν!) — the Liturgy announces not merely a text but the divine Wisdom/Logos/Christ, present and speaking.
  • Trisagion — sung before the Epistle; attributed in tradition to a vision of the angelic liturgy — the same heavenly worship seen by Isaiah (6:3) and Ezekiel. The Liturgy is the continuation of what the prophets witnessed.
  • Psalm 104/103 at Vespers — "who rides on the clouds as on a chariot" (v. 3) — cloud-riding imagery exclusively divine, belonging to Yahweh/Christ. Vespers opens with this proclamation every evening.
  • The Rublev Trinity icon — the Hospitality of Abraham as an image of the Holy Trinity, compressing the chapter's argument into a single iconographic tradition.
  • The Anaphora — explicitly identifies the Son as God's "Word" through whom all things were made (John 1:3; Prov. 8:30), and the Spirit as "Life-giving," present at Creation (Gen. 1:2).

Ascetic Formation

  • Reading Scripture as encounter — if "the Word of the Lord came to" the prophets describes a personal, bodily appearance of Christ, then OT lectio divina is not merely studying ancient texts but entering the company of those who were visited by the pre-incarnate Son.
  • The Shema as formation — St. Paul's modification of the Shema (1 Cor. 8:6) inserts Christ into Israel's most intimate prayer. The Trisagion and Lord's Prayer are the Christian equivalent formative prayers.
  • Guarding against inner Marcionism — ascetic formation requires holding both: the God who called Gideon, destroyed Sodom, and wrestled Jacob is the same God who wept over Lazarus. Courage to face the whole Christ is itself a form of νῆψις (watchfulness).

Sacramental Theology

  • Baptism — triple immersion "in the Name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit" initiates the catechumen into the fullness of the God of Israel as He actually is: three hypostases, one Yahweh.
  • The Eucharist — the pre-incarnate Son was present at Creation as the divine Craftsman; He is present in the Eucharist as the incarnate Lord of Creation. Every Liturgy is simultaneously Creation, Covenant, Exodus, theophany, and Eschaton.
  • Chrismation — the gift of the Holy Spirit at Chrismation is the culmination of the Trinitarian revelation. The newly illumined receives the Third Person of the Yahweh that Abraham entertained at Mamre.
  • The Liturgy of the Word — every Scripture proclamation is sacramental: the Living Word speaking through written words, a theophany of the same Person who appeared to Jeremiah and Isaiah.

Patristic Harmony

"God begot before all creatures a Beginning, a certain rational power from Himself, who is called by the Holy Spirit sometimes the Glory of the Lord, others the Son, others Wisdom, others an Angel, then God, and then Lord and Logos." — St. Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho, 61
  • St. Paul (1 Cor. 8:6; Phil. 2:5–11; 1 Cor. 10:3–4): Christology fully formed in the earliest NT documents — the opposite of what developmental theories predict.
  • St. Basil the Great and St. Gregory the Theologian: their defense of Nicene Trinitarianism was faithfulness to this same tradition, not innovation.
  • St. Jude (v. 5): "Jesus saved a people out of Egypt" — patristic witness that this identification was the original apostolic reading, not a late development.

Thematic Concept Analysis

Theme 1: Continuity of Revelation

The New Testament does not introduce a new God but reveals the same God of Israel in greater fullness. De Young establishes this through five parallel figures — Angel of the Lord, Word of the Lord, Wisdom of God, Son of Man, and bodily appearances — all identified by NT authors as the same Person: Jesus Christ. Soteriological implication: salvation history is one covenant in progressive unfolding; salvation has always been participation in the life of the triune Yahweh.

Theme 2: The Second Power in Heaven

The pre-Christian Jewish doctrine, documented in Second Temple literature, that Yahweh exists as two (or more) Persons — the Father and the "second power" who was seen, spoke, and acted in the world. De Young cites Alan Segal's Two Powers in Heaven and the Babylonian Talmud tractate Hagigah 14a (where Rabbi Akiva himself needed correction on this). Implication: the Incarnation is the entry of this eternally existent divine Person into created human nature — not the divinization of a man.

Theme 3: The Invention of Unitarian Monotheism

Strict unitarian monotheism ("God is one Person") characteristic of Rabbinic Judaism was a 2nd-century innovation — a reaction against Christianity, not original OT religion. Sequence: (1) OT presents multiple hypostases → (2) Second Temple Judaism debates their identity → (3) Christianity clarifies them as Father, Son, Spirit → (4) Rabbinic Judaism declares the "second power" heretical → (5) later additions (Maimonides' 13th Principle) codify this reaction.

Theme 4: The Developmental Hypothesis as Apologetic Target

The scholarly view (Ehrman, Harnack, Bultmann) that early Christians had "low Christology" and gradually divinized Jesus is refuted primarily through St. Paul's Epistles — the earliest NT writings — which contain fully formed Trinitarian Christology. Apologetic implication: Rabbinic Judaism evolved away from Israel's original multi-personal theism. Christianity is the conservative position in this historical dispute.

Theme 5: Christ as the God Seen in the Old Testament

The patristic principle that all OT theophanies were appearances of the pre-incarnate Son, not the Father — the Incarnation is therefore the definitive and final appearance of the same Person who had been appearing throughout Israel's history. Iconographic implication: we can paint Christ because He has always been seeable. The Incarnation gave permanent, human form to the One who had always appeared bodily to the Fathers.

Hypostasis ὑπόστασις A concrete, particular being or Person; not merely a mode or aspect of God The Nicene term for the three Persons of the Trinity; rooted in OT evidence for multiple divine Persons in Yahweh
Angel of the Lord Malakh Yahweh (Heb.) The divine messenger-figure simultaneously identified as Yahweh and distinct from another hypostasis Pre-incarnate Son; His OT appearances are the earliest evidence of multiple hypostases in the Godhead

Logos Λόγος The Word; the second Person of the Trinity as rational principle and agent of Creation St. John's prologue maps Logos onto the OT Debar Yahweh/Memra — a Jewish category, not a Greek philosophical import
Son of Man Bar Enash (Aram.) The divine-human figure of Daniel 7 who rides the clouds and receives all authority from the Ancient of Days Christ's primary self-designation; claiming this title at His trial was recognized as a claim to divinity (blasphemy)
Shema — "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One" — Israel's central confession of faith St. Paul modifies the Shema (1 Cor. 8:6) to include Christ as the "Lord," identifying Him as the second hypostasis
Theophany θεοφάνεια An appearance of God in bodily or visible form All OT theophanies are identified patristically as appearances of the pre-incarnate Son; they ground Orthodox iconography
Carmen Christi ὕμνος τοῦ Χριστοῦ The "Hymn to Christ" in Philippians 2:5–11; possibly pre-Pauline The earliest Christological text presents Christ as pre-existent divine Person — fully-formed theology within years of the Resurrection
Morphē Theou μορφὴ θεοῦ "Divine form" — the eternal state of Christ before the Incarnation (Phil. 2:6) Grammatically parallel to morphē doulou (form of a servant); if both are equally real, Christ is equally and truly God and man
Kenōsis κένωσις Self-emptying — the voluntary act by which the eternal Son took on human form Not a loss of divinity but a veiling of divine glory; grounds Orthodox ascetic theology: voluntary humility as participation in Christ's condescension
Apokalypsis Ἀποκάλυψις Revelation, uncovering — the title and genre of the Book of Revelation The book is a revelation of who Jesus Christ is, not primarily a timeline of future events
Alpha and Omega Ἄλφα καὶ Ὠμέγα First and last letters of the Greek alphabet; divine title for absolute sovereignty Applied identically to the Father (Rev. 1:8) and to Christ (Rev. 22:13–16) — one title, one divine nature, two Persons
Soft Marcionism — The tendency to accept a loving Christ while rejecting or ignoring His wrath and OT continuity Produces a partial Christ incapable of generating genuine repentance; countered by Heb. 13:8 and the wrath of the Lamb (Rev. 6:16)
Second Temple Judaism — Jewish religious practice from the rebuilding of the Temple (~516 BC) to its destruction (70 AD) The background world of Jesus and the Apostles; its literature widely attests belief in multiple divine hypostases before Christianity

Reflection Questions

Comprehension

  • What is the "assumed narrative" about Trinitarian development that De Young challenges? What are the three stages of that narrative and why does he say it is false?
  • Describe the five OT figures or categories that De Young identifies as evidence for multiple divine hypostases. What do they have in common?

Theological / Analytical

  • How does 1 Corinthians 8:6 (the modified Shema) refute the developmental hypothesis? What would developmental theory have predicted instead?
  • The chapter distinguishes between St. John's identification of the Logos (personal witness) and Philo's Logos theology (philosophical integration). Why does this distinction matter for how we read the NT prologue?

Personal / Devotional

  • Has your reading of the Old Testament been shaped by the assumption that it reveals a different or lesser God than the New Testament? How does this chapter challenge that assumption?
  • De Young warns against "soft Marcionism" — accepting the loving Christ while distancing from the wrathful God of the OT. Where do you notice this tendency in yourself or in the Christian culture around you? What does it cost theologically and spiritually?

Liturgical / Sacramental

  • De Young identifies the Hospitality of Abraham (Rublev's Trinity icon) as the beginning of the Trinitarian revelation, culminated at Pentecost. How does this icon function in Orthodox liturgical life? What does knowing its theological background add?
  • Given the chapter's argument that Wisdom/Sophia is a divine hypostasis — the pre-incarnate Christ — how does "Wisdom! Let us be attentive!" (Σοφία! Πρόσχωμεν!) change how you hear that proclamation? What posture does it call forth?

New Testament Christology

  • How does 1 Corinthians 8:6 demonstrate that St. Paul's Christology was fully formed in the earliest NT documents? What would St. Paul have had to not know for the developmental hypothesis to be plausible?
  • Revelation presents the Divine Liturgy as participation in the heavenly worship before the throne of God and of the Lamb. At your next Liturgy — or next time you pray the Hours — where do you notice the imagery of Revelation: the throne, the Lamb, the Alpha and Omega?
